<div id="s0001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i3" class="section-heading-2">Introduction</h2>
 <p>Infection of <i>Caenorhabditis elegans</i> induces broad changes in gene expression (reviewed in [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0001" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0002" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2</a></span>]). These are characterized by pathogen-specific responses and more generic changes [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0003" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>3</a></span>]. We previously noted among the genes induced by infection with the fungi <i>Drechmeria coniospora</i> an overrepresentation of genes repressed after infection by the bacterial pathogens <i>Serratia marcescens</i>, <i>Enterococcus faecalis</i> or <i>Photorhabdus luminescens</i>. Conversely, there was an overrepresentation of genes up-regulated upon infection by each of these bacterial pathogens among the genes down-regulated upon infection with <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora </i>[<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0004" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>4</a></span>]. Since <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> infects via the epidermis, while the bacterial pathogens colonize the nematode intestine, one possible interpretation of these results is that there is a communication between tissues. Thus, when immune defenses are activated in the epidermis, they would be repressed in the intestine and vice versa. In a parsimonious model, fungal infection of the epidermis or bacterial infection of the intestine would provoke an increase in the expression of a signaling molecule that would then modulate gene expression in other tissues.</p>
 <p>There are countless instances of such non-cell autonomous control of gene expression across all multicellular species. A number have been characterized in the context of the response of <i>C.&nbsp;elegans</i> to biotic and abiotic insults (reviewed in [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0005" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>5</a></span>]). To give one recent example, activation of a conserved p38 MAPK pathway in the intestine by rotenone is associated with protection from neurodegeneration [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0006" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span>], conceivably because induction of the mitochondrial unfolded protein response in the intestine represses the expression of the antimicrobial peptide gene <i>nlp-29</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0007" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>7</a></span>], which regulates dendrite degeneration in aging and infection [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0008" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>8</a></span>]. But, as is commonly the case, the molecular nature of the signal mediating this cross-tissue control has not yet been established. One prominent exception to this is the complex network of insulin signaling. With 40 genes encoding insulin-like peptides [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0009" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>9</a></span>], acting through a common conserved pathway to regulate the activity of the FOXO transcription factor DAF-16 and thereby the expression of hundreds of target genes across all tissues [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0010" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>10</a></span>], insulin signaling influences many if not all aspects of <i>C.&nbsp;elegans</i> physiology, including diapause, longevity, stress resistance and fertility [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0009" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>9</a></span>]. It has been demonstrated to play a role in innate immunity too. Specifically, the insulin-like peptide INS-7 from neurons has been shown to inhibit DAF-16 signaling in the intestine and thereby negatively-regulate pathogen resistance [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0011" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>11</a></span>]. Interestingly, infection with <i>Pseudomonas aeruginosa</i> provokes an increase in neuronal <i>ins-7</i> expression, leading to the down-regulation of putative immune defense genes in the intestine [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0012" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>12</a></span>]. And recently, a role in learning aversive behavior has been proposed for insulin-like peptide INS-11 upon infection by <i>P. aeruginosa</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span>]. In this study, we investigated the induction of <i>ins-11</i> caused by different infections and propose a non-cell autonomous role for <i>ins-11</i> in cross-tissue communication.</p>
</div>
<div id="s0002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i4" class="section-heading-2">Results and discussion</h2>
 <div id="s0002-s2001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i5">Expression of insulin peptides upon infection</h3>
  <p>Given their known roles in cross-tissue communication and in innate immune regulation, insulin-like peptides are prime candidates for mediating the reciprocal control of epidermal and intestinal gene expression seen following infection of <i>C.&nbsp;elegans</i>. We therefore first reviewed data from our previous studies to identify insulin-like genes that are induced both by fungal and bacterial pathogens. Infection of <i>C.&nbsp;elegans</i> by <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> provokes a marked increase in the expression of 4 insulin-like genes, <i>ins-7</i>, <i>ins-11</i>, <i>ins-23</i> and <i>ins-36</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0004" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>4</a></span>]. Among them, although <i>ins-7</i> expression is induced by <i>P.&nbsp;aeruginosa</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0012" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>12</a></span>], it did not figure among the list of genes differentially regulated upon infection by the bacterial pathogens <i>S.&nbsp;marcescens</i>, <i>E.&nbsp;faecalis</i> or <i>P.&nbsp;luminescens</i>. And while the expression of <i>ins-23</i> and <i>ins-36</i> was decreased upon infection with <i>S.&nbsp;marcescens</i>, for <i>ins-11</i> it was increased, as it was also following infection with <i>E.&nbsp;faecalis</i> and <i>P.&nbsp;luminescens</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0004" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>4</a></span>]. Further, other investigators have reported an induction of <i>ins-11</i> expression after infection with <i>Bacillus thuringiensis</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0014" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>14</a></span>], <i>P. aeruginosa</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0015" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>15</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0016" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>16</a></span>], <i>Staphylococcus aureus</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0017" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>17</a></span>] and the microsporidian species <i>Nematocida parisii</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0018" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>18</a></span>]. We therefore focused our attention on <i>ins-11</i> and its potential role in mediating a cross-tissue regulation of innate immune genes.</p>
 </div>
 <div id="s0002-s2002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i6"><i>hlh-30</i> dependent upregulation of <i>ins-11</i> in the intestine upon bacterial infection</h3>
  <p>As a first step in the characterization of <i>ins-11</i>, we verified its expression upon infection by <i>S.&nbsp;marcescens.</i> By qRT-PCR we observed a clear induction of <i>ins-11</i> gene expression, as we did for the C-type lectin gene <i>clec-67</i>, a putative defense gene that is expressed in the intestine and is up-regulated by infection by diverse bacteria [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0003" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>3</a></span>] (<a href="#f0001">Fig. 1A</a>). It has previously been shown that following <i>S.&nbsp;aureus</i> or <i>P. aeruginosa</i> infection, <i>ins-11</i> expression requires the basic helix-loop-helix transcription factor HLH-30 [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0017" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>17</a></span>]. In the case of infection by <i>S.&nbsp;marcescens</i>, <i>ins-11</i> expression was also dependent upon <i>hlh-30</i> (<a href="#f0001">Fig. 1A</a>). As previously reported [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0019" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>19</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0020" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>20</a></span>], a strain carrying an <i>ins-11</i> transcriptional reporter construct (<i>ins-11p::GFP</i>) exhibited only a low constitutive fluorescence in adults, in the nerve ring, labial neurons and most obviously in the intestine (<a href="#f0001">Fig. 1B</a>). When this strain was infected by <i>S.&nbsp;marcescens</i>, a clear increase in GFP expression was observed in the intestine (<a href="#f0001">Fig. 1C</a>). Interestingly, the same type of induction in the intestine was also recently reported upon infection by <i>P. aeruginosa</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span>]. This suggests that diverse bacterial intestinal infections can trigger the up-regulation of <i>ins-11</i> in the intestine.</p>

 <div id="s0002-s2003" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i8"><i>sta-2</i> dependent upregulation of <i>ins-11</i> in the epidermis upon fungal infection</h3>
  <p>We then assayed the expression of <i>ins-11</i> upon infection by <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> by qRT-PCR, and confirmed its strong induction, of the same order of magnitude as that of the well-characterized antimicrobial peptide gene <i>nlp-29</i> (<a href="#f0002">Fig. 2A</a>). Infecting a strain carrying an <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> reporter with <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> provokes GFP expression in the epidermis, particularly at the perivulval cells when spores adhered to this region of the adult cuticle [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0021" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>21</a></span>]. A very similar pattern was seen when we infected worms carry the <i>ins-11p::GFP</i> reporter (<a href="#f0002">Fig. 2B, C</a>). It is notable that epidermal expression of <i>ins-11</i> has not previously been reported [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0019" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>19</a></span>]. Strong GFP expression was also observed in the epidermis when the <i>ins-11p::GFP</i> reporter was transferred into a strain that expresses a constitutively active Gα protein, GPA-12#, in the adult epidermis (<a href="#f0002">Fig. 2D</a>); <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> expression is similarly elevated in the GPA-12# background [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0022" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>22</a></span>]. GPA-12 acts upstream of the PMK-1 p38 MAPK cascade to regulate <i>nlp-29</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0023" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>23</a></span>]. Our result suggests that <i>ins-11</i> expression in the epidermis might be controlled in the same manner as <i>nlp-29</i>. While <i>hlh-30</i> is dispensable for <i>nlp-29</i> expression following <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> infection (<sup>7</sup> and results not shown), the STAT-like transcription factor gene <i>sta-2</i> has been shown to have a specific function in the regulation of antifungal defenses in the epidermis, downstream of the GPA-12/PMK-1 pathway [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0024" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>24</a></span>]. Similarly, we found the induction of <i>ins-11</i> upon infection by <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> to depend very largely upon <i>sta-2</i> when we assayed the level of the endogenous gene by qRT-PCR (<a href="#f0002">Fig. 2A</a>) or the fluorescence level of the reporter strain (<a href="#f0002">Fig. 2B</a>). Our results therefore suggest that <i>ins-11</i> expression is triggered in infected tissues, and regulated in the epidermis and intestine by distinct transcription factors.</p>

 <div id="s0002-s2004" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i10">Cell autonomous inactivation of p38 MAPK PMK-1</h3>
  <p>To investigate the regulation of <i>ins-11</i> in different tissues, we made use of the <i>Shigella flexneri</i> protein effector OspF that irreversibly dephosphorylates p38 [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0025" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>25</a></span>] since this offers a straightforward way to affect signaling in a temporally-controlled and tissue-specific manner. OspF is a dual-specificity phosphatase and also dephosphorylates ERK MAPKs. While the nematode ERK pathway involving the MAPK MPK-1 has been shown to be important for the rectal epithelial cell swelling response to <i>Microbacterium nematophilum</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0026" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>26</a></span>] and for the swelling response to <i>Staphylococcus aureus</i>, it is dispensable for the intestinal transcriptional response [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0027" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>27</a></span>]. The ERK pathway is not involved in the response to <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> either [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0007" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>7</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0028" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>28</a></span>], so if OspF has an effect on infection-induced gene expression, it is expected to be solely via the p38 MAPK pathway. Since we have previously observed unexpected changes in gene expression patterns when we interfered with p38 signaling in a single tissue [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0007" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>7</a></span>], we used the transcriptional reporters described above to assay the effect of tissue-specific inhibition of MAPK activity. Expression of OspF in the intestine, under the control of the <i>vit-6</i> promoter, had little or no effect on the induction of <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> expression that normally accompanies infection by <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i>. When OspF was expressed under the control of an adult epidermis-specific promoter (<i>col-19p</i>), on the other hand, there was a very marked reduction in <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> expression (<a href="#f0003">Fig. 3A, B</a>). These results are consistent with our previous findings that <i>nlp-29</i> is controlled in the epidermis in a cell autonomous manner by the p38 MAPK PMK-1 [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0029" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>29</a></span>]. Expressing OspF in the intestine did abrogate <i>ins-11p::GFP</i> expression in the intestine following infection with <i>S.&nbsp;marcescens</i> expression, while its expression in the epidermis strongly inhibited <i>ins-11</i> reporter gene expression upon <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> infection (<a href="#f0003">Fig. 3C, D</a>). Together, these results suggest that <i>ins-11</i> expression is controlled cell autonomously by p38 MAPK signaling both in the epidermis and in the intestine, via distinct downstream transcription factors (STA-2 and HLH-30).</p>

  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i12">No cell autonomous role of <i>ins-11</i> or <i>daf-16</i> upon infection</h3>
  <p>Zou et al. reported that infection by <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> leads to nuclear translocation of the FOXO transcription factor DAF-16 [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0030" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>30</a></span>]. With the <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> strain [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0031" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>31</a></span>] used in this and all our previous studies (ATCC 96282), and the DAF-16::GFP reporter protein used by Zou et al., we failed, however, to observe any such translocation. DAF-16 also remained cytoplasmic in the strain expressing GPA-12# in the epidermis that mimics <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> infection (see below; results not shown). Further, fungal infection did not interfere with the normal cytoplasm-nucleus shuttling of DAF-16 associated with transient heat-shock [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0032" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>32</a></span>] (<a href="#f0004">Fig. 4A</a>). Thus contrary to the results of Zou <i>et al</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0030" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>30</a></span>], we have no evidence to suggest that infection by <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> leads to nuclear translocation of DAF-16. As we have not been able to procure the strain used by Zou <i>et al</i>., nor garner any information about it, the reason for the discrepancy is unclear.</p>

  <p>We further tested <i>daf-2(ts)</i> mutant worms in which DAF-16 is constitutively active at the restrictive temperature (25 °C) with a predominantly nuclear localization [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0032" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>32</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0033" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>33</a></span>]. In the <i>daf-2</i> mutant background, there was a marked reduction of the induction of the antimicrobial peptide reporter gene <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> compared to wild-type worms that was suppressed by <i>daf-16</i> inactivation (<a href="#f0004">Fig. 4B</a>). <i>daf-2</i> mutants have an altered cuticle [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0034" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>34</a></span>]. Changes in the surface coat or cuticle can affect the binding of fungal spores [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0007" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>7</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0035" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>35</a></span>]. We found that the reduced induction of <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> correlated with decreased binding of <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> spores to the <i>daf-2</i> mutant cuticle (<a href="#f0004">Fig. 4C</a>). These results serve as a caveat for studies using these mutants. To circumvent the changes in cuticle in <i>daf-2</i> mutants, we used starvation to drive DAF-16 into the nucleus [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0032" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>32</a></span>] in young adult worms. In this case, the induction of <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> expression upon infection was normal (<a href="#f0004">Fig. 4D</a>). Taken together, our results suggest that the insulin signaling pathway is unlikely to impinge directly on the cell autonomous intracellular p38 MAPK pathway known to control <i>nlp</i> antimicrobial peptide gene expression.</p>

 <div id="s0002-s2006" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i14">Activation of GPA-12 in the epidermis mimics <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> infection at the transcriptional level</h3>
  <p>To determine whether <i>ins-11</i> might play a role in innate immunity by regulating the expression of defense genes other than <i>nlp-29</i>, we conducted an exploratory transcriptomic study using RNA-seq to compare the transcriptional profiles of wild type, and GPA-12#-expressing animals, with and without <i>ins-11</i>. Expression was detected for 19,845 genes in at least one condition. Principle component analyses neatly separated the two “treatments”, loss of <i>ins-11</i> function and gain of function of <i>gpa-12</i>, with the first 2 components accounting for most of the variability (49 % and 27 %; Table S1). As expected, class enrichment analysis [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0007" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>7</a></span>]; bioinformatics.lif.univ-mrs.fr/YAAT; Thakur et al. in preparation) showed a very substantial overlap (117/252 genes; 46 %; p&nbsp;&lt;&nbsp;10<sup>−67</sup>) between the list of genes up-regulated in GPA-12# worms (relative to N2) and the list of genes up-regulated upon <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> infection [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0004" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>4</a></span>], including <i>ins-11</i> (&gt; 25-fold), and more than 20 genes that potentially encode AMPs [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0036" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>36</a></span>] (with 8 <i>cnc</i>, 5 <i>fip</i> and <i>fipr</i>, 7 <i>nlp</i> genes; Table S1). It also identified a number of other categories previously noted to be enriched for innate immune genes. This includes genes regulated by <i>nhr-25</i> and <i>acs-3</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0037" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>37</a></span>], <i>sam-10</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0038" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>38</a></span>] or <i>dapk-1</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0039" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>39</a></span>], induced by infection with <i>Burkholderia pseudomallei</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0040" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>40</a></span>] and in a series of mutants with an alteration of their cuticle and/or osmotic balance (<i>osm-7</i>, <i>osm-8</i>, <i>osm-11</i>) [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0041" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>41</a></span>]. As expected, there was also a very significant enrichment (p&nbsp;&lt;&nbsp;10<sup>−28</sup>) for genes characterized as being expressed in the epidermis [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0042" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>42</a></span>] (Table S1). Interestingly, regarding the genes down-regulated both by infection and in the GPA-12# background, the overlap was proportionally smaller (104/653) although still significant (p &lt; 4.5&nbsp;× 10<sup>-4</sup>), and there were comparatively fewer epidermally-expressed genes (33/104&nbsp;vs 61/117). One could speculate that <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> might be able to down-regulate host gene expression through the action of its many candidate secreted virulence factors [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0043" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>43</a></span>]. These analyses nevertheless validate the use of strains carrying <i>col-19p::GPA-12#</i> as a model for the inductive part of the epidermal innate immune response.</p>
 </div>
 <div id="s0002-s2007" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i15">A potential non cell-autonomous role of <i>ins-11</i> in transcriptional regulation</h3>
  <p>Since the expression of <i>ins-11</i> is modulated by the GPA-12/p38 MAPK cascade that is central to the transcriptional response to <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> infection, any gene that is a target of <i>ins-11</i> regulation should also appear to be regulated in a concordant manner by <i>D.&nbsp;coniospora</i> infection and the GPA-12/p38 MAPK cascade. Comparing the list of genes that were up regulated by expression of GPA-12# in the wild-type versus <i>ins-11(0)</i> background, we observed 130 genes in common (34%) that are thus targets of the p38 cascade, regulated in a manner independent of <i>ins-11</i> (<a href="#f0005">Fig. 5A</a>, Table S2). Consistent with our results with the <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> reporter gene above, these included <i>nlp</i> and <i>cnc</i> genes, which encode secreted AMPs, and other genes known to be expressed in the epidermis (as mentioned above [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0044" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>44</a></span>]). In line with this, among the 8 Gene Ontology (GO) terms returned by class enrichment analysis [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0007" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>7</a></span>]; Thakur et al. in preparation), the one with the highest probability was “defense response to fungus” (GO:0050832; <a href="#f0005">Fig. 5A</a>; Table S2). On the other hand, 122 genes were found to be up-regulated only in the wild-type (32%) and their expression is thus potentially dependent on <i>ins-11</i>. Interestingly, the list included genes known to be up-regulated in the intestine by infection (like <i>irg-4</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0045" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>45</a></span>], <i>irg-5</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0046" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>46</a></span>], and <i>kreg-1</i> [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0047" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>47</a></span>]) or abiotic stress like glycosyltransferase (<i>ugt</i>) or cytochrome P450 (<i>cyp</i>) genes; the associated GO terms included “innate immune response” (GO:0045087). A last group of 127 genes (34%) were only found to be up-regulated in the <i>ins-11(0)</i> mutant background, so these are potentially repressed by <i>ins-11</i> upon GPA-12 activation. The only significantly enriched GO term, “defense response to Gram-positive bacterium”, (GO:0050830) reflected the many intestinal genes differentially regulated by <i>Bacillus thuringiensis</i> (such as <i>lys-7</i>; see below), but the list also included genes expressed in the epidermis such as collagen, cuticulin, astacin and patched-like genes [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0042" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>42</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0044" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>44</a></span>].</p>

  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i17"><i>ins-11</i> expression in the young adult epidermis affects longevity</h3>
  <p>As mentioned above, <i>ins-11</i> is not strongly expressed under normal laboratory conditions and only mild developmental and physiological phenotypes have been observed in the <i>ins-11</i> mutant: increased fat accumulation in the adult [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0052" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>52</a></span>] and an increase in the number of eggs laid [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span>]. On the other hand, <i>ins-11</i> is expressed in the epidermis upon fungal infection, and under these conditions its loss might be expected to have more marked consequences. We therefore decided to assay the effect of the loss of the <i>ins-11</i> in a strain carrying the gain of function Gα protein (GPA-12#) mentioned above that mimics infection. Under these conditions, we observed a small but significant increase in the rate of egg laying upon loss of <i>ins-11</i>. Since this is similar to the increase recently reported in the wild-type background [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span>], it indicates that <i>ins-11</i> expression in the epidermis does not have a major influence on this phenotype (<a href="#f0006">Fig. 6A</a>). We also noted a small but significant difference in the size of adult GPA-12# worms upon loss of <i>ins-11</i>, but again, as this effect was seen when comparing <i>ins-11</i> mutants and wild type worms, this phenotype is a not primarily a consequence of expression of <i>ins-11</i> in the adult epidermis (<a href="#f0006">Fig. 6B</a> &amp; Fig. S1).</p>

  <p></p>
  <p>Given the opposing effect of <i>ins-11</i> that we observed for immune defense gene expression (<a href="#f0006">Fig. 6C</a>, Table S2), it is difficult to predict what effect <i>ins-11</i> would have on the capacity of worms to resist infection. Indeed, no difference was reported during <i>P.&nbsp;aeruginosa</i> infection between the wild type and the <i>ins-11</i> mutant [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span>], and preliminary tests indicated no significant difference in the survival of adult GPA-12# worms upon loss of <i>ins-11</i> following infection with <i>S.&nbsp;marcescens</i> or <i>P.&nbsp;aeruginosa</i> (data not shown). In both cases, however, the progression of the infection is relatively rapid. In order to assay the effect of <i>ins-11</i> in a less acute context, we therefore measured the lifespan of the 2 strains in the absence of infection. We observed an increase in the longevity of the <i>ins-11</i>;GPA-12# worms relative to the GPA-12# controls (<a href="#f0006">Fig. 6C</a>, p value &lt; 0.0001, median survival 149 and 173&nbsp;hours for <i>wt;</i>GPA-12# and <i>ins-11;</i>GPA-12# strains, respectively). As no difference was observed between wild type and <i>ins-11</i> mutant worms cultured on the regular non-pathogenic OP50 bacteria [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0052" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>52</a></span>], our data suggest that expression of <i>ins-11</i> in the adult epidermis has a negative effect on longevity. A previous report suggested that <i>ins-11</i> expression has a deleterious effect on the longevity of adult hermaphrodite worms in the presence of males [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0053" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>53</a></span>]. One can speculate that mating provokes epidermal wounding in hermaphrodites and therefore an induction of <i>ins-11</i> in the epidermis, as we observed in the GPA-12# transgenic worms. It would be interesting to test this hypothesis in the future.</p>
  <p>Taken together, our results suggest that activation of the p38 MAPK pathway in the epidermis of <i>C.&nbsp;elegans</i> leads to an increase in cell autonomous defenses (e.g. AMP gene expression), but also impacts on intestinal gene expression, in part through the production of the insulin-like peptide INS-11. Although the effect of increased INS-11 levels is complex with regard the expression of individual intestinal defense genes, one consequence is that worms have a reduced longevity. We speculate that this reflects an altered allocation of energy, with resources being directed to the tissue most at threat from a pathogen. Interestingly, upon bacterial infection <i>ins-11</i> is induced in the intestine to counter balance a protective aversive behavior probably to limit the time spend out of food [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span>]. Notably, in <i>Drosophila</i>, induction of an insulin-like peptide is used during development to coordinate organ growth [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0054" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>54</a></span>]. It would be interesting in the future to investigate whether <i>ins-11</i> serves as a sentinel during development, alerting the organism when tissue homeostasis is perturbed, by infection or other insults, thus promoting optimal growth even under conditions of stress.</p>

 <div id="s0003-s2002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i21">Constructs and transgenic lines</h3>
  <p>All the transgenic strains carrying <i>frIs7</i>, <i>frSi2</i> or <i>wwEx70</i> were obtained by conventional crosses and all genotypes were confirmed by PCR or sequencing. All constructs were made using Gibson Assembly (NEB Inc., MA).</p>
  <p><i>frSi2</i> is a single copy insertion on chromosome II at the location of the Nemagenetag <i>Mos1</i> insertion [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0057" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>57</a></span>] <i>ttTi5605</i> of pNP138 (<i>col-19p::GPA-12#</i>). pNP138 was obtained by insertion of the <i>col-19</i> promoter fused to the DNA encoding an activated form of GPA-12# (with the Q205L mutation) [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0022" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>22</a></span>] into the MosSCI vector pCFJ151 [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0058" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>58</a></span>]. It was injected (at the Lyon platform UMS3421 CNRS) into the EG6699 strain at 30 ng/µl together with pGH8 (<i>rab-3p::mCherry</i>) at 10 ng/µl, pCFJ90 (<i>myo-2p::mCherry</i>) at 2.5 ng/µl, pCFJ104 (<i>myo-3p::mCherry</i>) at 5 ng/µl, pMA122 (<i>hsp16.41p::PEEL-1</i>) at 10 ng/µl, pCFJ601 (<i>eft-3p::Mos1</i> transposase) at 50 ng/µl and pBluescript at 50 ng/µl. A strain containing the insertion was obtained following standard selection and PCR confirmation [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0058" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>58</a></span>].</p>
  <p><i>frSi14</i> and <i>frSi15</i> are single copy insertions on chromosome I at the location of the Nemagenetag <i>Mos1</i> insertion <i>ttTi4348</i> of the pSO9 (<i>vit-6p::OspF::unc-54 3′UTR</i>) and the pSO7 (<i>col-19p::OspF::unc-54 3′UTR</i>) plasmids, respectively. They were obtained by CRISPR using a self-excising cassette (SEC) [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0059" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>59</a></span>] that was modified for single insertions on chromosome I. pSO9 and pSO7 were obtained by insertion of either the intestinal-specific promoter <i>vit-6</i> or the epidermis-specific promoter <i>col-19</i> fused to the gene for the <i>Shigella flexneri</i> protein effector OspF that irreversibly dephosphorylates p38 [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0025" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>25</a></span>] into the pSO5.3 vector. pSO5.3 was made from a vector containing the SEC for single insertion on Chromosome II at the position of <i>ttTi5605</i> (pAP087, kindly provided by Ari Pani), changing both homology arms to target chromosome I at the position of <i>ttTi4348</i>. pSO9 and pSO7 were injected in N2 at 10 ng/µl together with pDD122 (<i>eft-3p::Cas9</i>) at 40 ng/µl, pCFJ90 (<i>myo-2p::mCherry</i>) at 2.5 ng/µl, pCFJ104 (<i>myo-3p::mCherry</i>) at 5 ng/µl, and pCZGY2747 (sgRNA ttTi4348) at 30 ng/µl (kindly provided by Yishi Jin). Non-fluorescent roller worms were selected then heat shocked to remove the SEC by FloxP as described in [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0059" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>59</a></span>]. Plasmid and primers sequences are available upon request.</p>
 </div>
 <div id="s0003-s2003" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i22">Infection</h3>
  <p>Eggs prepared by the standard bleach method were allowed to hatch in 50&nbsp;mM NaCl in the absence of food at 25 °C. Synchronized L1 larvae were transferred to NGM agar plates spread with <i>E. coli</i> OP50. N2 worms were cultured at 25 °C until the young adult stage (45 hours) and then transferred to plates spread with <i>S. marcescens</i> Db10 which had been cultured in liquid LB at 37 °C overnight, put on NGM plates for 24&nbsp;hours at 25 °C, then cultured another 20&nbsp;hours at 25 °C as described [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0004" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>4</a></span>]. For infection of <i>D. coniospora</i>, L1 larvae were cultured at 25 °C until the L4 stage (40 hours) before being exposed to fungal spores on standard NGM plate spread with OP50, as previously described [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0060" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>60</a></span>]. Both infection assays were done on a standard small lawn of bacteria. For infection upon starvation, worms were harvested at the young adult stage, starved for 14&nbsp;hours on NGM plates with carbenicillin then infected for 6&nbsp;hours. To observe the effect of infection on the translocation of DAF-16, TJ356 worms were first infected for 13&nbsp;hours then heat-shocked for 30&nbsp;min at 37 °C and observed directly and then again after a period of one hour at 25 °C. To count the number of spores, young adult worms were infected for 8&nbsp;hours at 25 °C then observed on a slide under the microscope. Statistical significance was determined using a two-tailed unpaired student's T-test (Graphpad Prism).</p>
 </div>
 <div id="s0003-s2004" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i23">qRT-PCR</h3>
  <p>RNA samples were obtained following Trizol (Invitrogen)/ chloroform extraction and then diluted in nuclease-free water to a final concentration of 100 ng/µl. One µg of total RNA from infected or non-infected worms were used for reverse transcription (Applied Biosystems™). Quantitative real-time PCR were performed using 1 µl of cDNA in 10 µl of SYBR Green Applied Biosystem™ and 0.1 µM of primers on a 7500 Fast Real-Time PCR System using <i>act-1</i> as a control. All primers were designed to be specific for the cDNA (see Table S3 for the sequences). Normalized threshold cycle values were used to calculate fold increases or decreases of RNA levels in samples from test condition compared with controls. The <i>Ct</i> values were normalized to changes in <i>act-1</i> that were found to not change with infection. The details of the analysis procedure are described in [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0061" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>61</a></span>]. Mean and SEMs were calculated from a minimum of 3 independent experiments, statistical significance was determined using a one-tailed ratio paired student's T-test (Graphpad Prism).</p>
 </div>
 <div id="s0003-s2005" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i24">Fluorescent reporter analyses</h3>
  <p>Analysis of <i>nlp-29p::GFP</i> or <i>ins-11p::GFP</i> expression was quantified with the COPAS Biosort (Union Biometrica; Holliston, MA) as described in [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0029" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>29</a></span>]. In each case, the results are representative of at least 3 independent experiments where more than 70 worms were analyzed. The fluorescent ratio between GFP and size is represented in arbitrary units. Statistical significance was determined using a non-parametric analysis of variance with a Dunn's test (Graphpad Prism). Fluorescent images were taken of transgenic worms mounted on a 2 % agarose pad on a glass slide anesthetized with 5&nbsp;mM levamisole, using the Zeiss AxioCam HR digital colour camera and AxioVision Rel. 4.6 software (Carl Zeiss AG).</p>
 </div>
 <div id="s0003-s2006" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i25">Egg laying and survival analysis</h3>
  <p>20 to 30 young adult (L4 + 12 hours) IG1564 (<i>wt; frSi2[col-19p::GPA-12#])</i> and IG1647 (<i>ins-11(tm1053); frSi2[col-19p::GPA-12#]</i>) worms were transferred to fresh NGM plates every 2&nbsp;hours for 3&nbsp;times over the course of the first day, with a special care being taken to put them inside the OP50 lawn. F1 worms were counted and analysed for their size using the COPAS worm sorter after 3&nbsp;days at 25 °C. 15 independent plates were analyzed for each strains. The number of F1 per hour and per adult was calculated for each plate. For survival analysis, the same young adult worms were used and transferred every day until they finished to lay eggs (no FUDR was used), then observed each day until they died [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0060" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>60</a></span>]. Statistical significance was determined using a non parametric two tailed student's T-test for the number of F1 progeny and their length and a Mantel-Cox log-rank test for survival (Graphpad Prism).</p>
 </div>
 <div id="s0003-s2007" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i26">RNA-seq analysis</h3>
  <p>Total RNA from synchronized young adult worms (N2 and <i>ins-11(tm1053)</i> worms, with or without the <i>frSi2</i> transgene) was obtained following Trizol (Invitrogen)/ chloroform extraction. The RNA sample was treated with DNAse using RNA clean up kit (Qiagen) then sent for RNA-sequencing (Illumina SE50, 20M reads/sample at Novogene, Beijing, China). The data analysis was done through an in-house standard analysis workflow for RNA-seq. In brief, clean reads were obtained by removing containing adapter sequences, reads containing poly-N and low quality reads. All the downstream analysis was done based on the clean reads with high quality. The single-end clean reads were then aligned to the reference <i>C.&nbsp;elegans</i> genome (genome sequence WS235; genome annotation WS260). The reads number mapped to each gene were counted and the expected number of fragments per kilo base of transcript sequence per millions base pairs sequenced of each gene was calculated based on the length of the gene and read count mapped to this gene. Finally, differential expression analysis was performed to determine the differentially expressed genes based on the selected P-value. Differentially-expressed genes were defined using the previously described algorithms [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0004" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>4</a></span>]. Genes were annotated using SimpleMine WS260, analysed using Venny [<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0062" data-reflink="_i27 _i29" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>62</a></span>], and class enrichment analyses used YAAT <a class="ext-link" href="http://bioinformatics.lif.univ-mrs.fr/YAAT/" target="_blank">http://bioinformatics.lif.univ-mrs.fr/YAAT/</a> (Thakur et al. in preparation).</p>
 </div>
