<div id="S0001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <p>The frequency of hip fractures is increasing with our ageing population, with an annual incidence of between 1.4 and 5 per 103 per year (Lonnroos et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0027" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>, Icks et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0016" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2008</a></span>, Varez-Nebreda et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0040" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2008</a></span>). Health model projections have estimated that 6.3 million hip fractures will occur annually worldwide within the next 40 years (Cooper et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0007" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1992</a></span>), imposing a significant economic health burden. There is a large reported perioperative mortality rate in this population, ranging from 2.4% to 8.2% at 1 month (Parvizi et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0031" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2001</a></span>, Radcliff et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0035" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2008</a></span>) and over 25% at 1 year (Elliott et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0009" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003</a></span>, Jiang et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0017" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005</a></span>). Furthermore, it was recently reported that the current mortality rate is higher now than 25 years ago (Vestergaard et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0041" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2007a</a></span>). Today, it is generally accepted that displaced intracapsular fractures are best treated with arthroplasty rather than internal fixation (Keating et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0019" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>, Leighton et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0025" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2007</a></span>). In the at-risk population, however, multiple comorbidities are common and the best form of component fixation is in question.</p>
 <p>Bone cement implantation syndrome is a well-described complication of cemented hip arthroplasty. It is characterized by a systemic drop in systolic blood pressure, hypoxemia, pulmonary hypertension, cardiac dysrhythmias, and occasionally cardiac arrest and death (Rinecker <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0037" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1980</a></span>, Orsini et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0028" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1987</a></span>, Parvizi et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0030" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1999</a></span>). The prevailing theory to explain the pathophysiology of this phenomenon is embolism of fat, marrow contents, bone, and to some degree methylmethacrylate to the lung (Rinecker <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0037" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1980</a></span>, Elmaraghy et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1998</a></span>, Parvizi et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0030" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1999</a></span>, Koessler et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0023" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2001</a></span>). An increased degree of pulmonary insult with fat microemboli has been demonstrated (mostly in randomized controlled trials) during insertion of a cemented femoral stem rather than an uncemented implant (Orsini et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0028" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1987</a></span>, Ries et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0036" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1993</a></span>, Christie et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0004" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1994</a></span>, Pitto et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0034" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1999</a></span>), presumably due to increased intramedullary femoral canal pressures in the cemented group (Kallos et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0018" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1974</a></span>, Orsini et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0028" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1987</a></span>). These pressures can be reduced by the use of distal venting holes in the femur during stem insertion (Engesæter et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0012" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1984</a></span>). It has been shown previously by single-institutional review that patients undergoing cemented hip arthroplasty have a higher intraoperative mortality rate relative to uncemented arthroplasty, presumably due to a reduced incidence of fat embolism in the latter group (Parvizi et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0030" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1999</a></span>). The increased mortality risk was also present at 30 days in the treatment of acute fractures with cemented arthroplasty, also from a single-institutional review (Parvizi et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0032" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span>). Although cement-related mortality is rare (Dearborn and Harris <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0008" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1998</a></span>, Parvizi et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0030" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1999</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0031" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2001</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0032" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span>, Weinrauch et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0043" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>), it is a devastating complication—often reported through observational studies or literature reviews. Proponents of uncemented hip arthroplasty often cite this concern to support their reluctance to use cemented hip arthroplasty in both elective procedures and fracture management. However, many different types of studies have been unable to identify any increased mortality risk with the use of cement (Lausten and Vedel <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0024" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1982</a></span> (observational), Emery et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1991</a></span> (RCT), Lo et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0026" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1994</a></span> (observational), Khan et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0020" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002a</a></span>,b (literature review), Parker and Gurusamy <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0029" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span> (literature review)) and others have shown a decrease in mortality at 30 days when cement is used (Foster et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0013" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005</a></span>).</p>
 <p>Cemented hip hemiarthroplasty appears to offer improved rate of return to baseline function, reduced postoperative pain, and superior long-term survivorship relative to uncemented arthroplasty (Khan et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0020" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002a</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0021" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>b</a></span>, Parker and Gurusamy <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0029" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span>). We reasoned that failure to return to baseline function after hemiarthroplasty may be another risk factor for perioperative mortality (Hannan et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0015" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2001</a></span>, Braithwaite et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0003" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003</a></span>). Lower revision rates for cemented prostheses and increased mortality at revision surgery contribute further to reducing the overall mortality risk. We evaluated the relationship between the method of fixation of hip arthroplasty and perioperative mortality using a large national joint replacement registry.</p>
</div>
<div id="S0002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i1" class="section-heading-2">Patients and methods</h2>
 <p>Data pertaining to patient age, implant type, fixation method, and patient location were obtained from the Australian Orthopaedic Association (AOA) National Joint Replacement Registry (NJRR). Mortality information was obtained by patient matching with the National Death Index (NDI) from the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare. The outcome of interest was mortality at 1 day, 1 week, 1 month, and 1 year after surgery. Data were then stratified by implant type to examine the effect of cement fixation within monoblock and modular implant procedures.</p>
 <p>The AOA NJRR identified patient selection differences for implant type based on demographic data. As patient comorbidities are not captured in the AOA NJRR, these demographics were used as a surrogate measure for different patient populations in an effort to adjust for bias in the comparison of fixation method. We hypothesized that monoblock components are usually reserved for more elderly, lower-demand patients with more comorbidities and that modular prosthesis implants are used in healthier patients with expected longer survival.</p>
 <p>Data in the AOA NJRR are collected at the time of surgery using a standard paper-based form, with methods described in more detail elsewhere (Conroy et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2008</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0001" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>AOA 2009</a></span>). Each hospital subsequently forwards these forms to the registry for data entry. Forms with incomplete or inconsistent data are followed up by the registry with the hospital concerned. Cases where forms have not been completed are identified by verification of registry data using government hospitalization separation data.</p>
 <div id="S0002-S2001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i2">Statistics</h3>
  <p>Mortality rates were compared between cemented and uncemented prostheses using a time-dependent Cox proportional-hazards model. For each model, the assumption of proportional hazards was checked analytically by inspecting the graph of log(log(survival)) plotted against log of survival time. Time points were selected a priori based on clinical importance, and hazard ratios were then calculated for each selected time period. All analyses were adjusted for age and sex as measured at the date of the primary procedure. All analyses were performed using SAS software version 9.1.</p>
 </div>
 <div id="S0002-S2002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i3">Ethics</h3>
  <p>Local ethical approval was not required from our institution, as this study was purely data-driven and used de-identified national data. A formal request was made to the Australian Orthopaedic Association (AOA) National Joint Replacement Registry (NJRR) for access to the national de-identified data.</p>
 </div>

  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i6">Perioperative mortality</h3>
  <p>Kaplan Meier survival estimates by postoperative days are shown in <a href="#F0001">Figure 1</a> and hazard ratios are detailed in <button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="T0002">Table 2</button>. There was an increased risk of perioperative mortality in patients treated with uncemented hemiarthroplasty at 1 week (p = 0.02), 1 month (p = 0.03), and 1 year (p &lt; 0.001) postoperatively. We were therefore interested in further characterizing the role of fixation method in different patient groups, to identify the true effect of cement on mortality.</p>

  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i8">Cemented vs. uncemented monoblock components</h3>

 <h2 id="_i13" class="section-heading-2">Discussion</h2>
 <p>Compared to uncemented procedures, we found reduced mortality at 1 year following a cemented procedure. At the outset of this study, it was our hypothesis that there would be similar mortality rates for cemented and uncemented hemiarthroplasty using a large, nationwide joint registry database. The fact that the data revealed a lower overall mortality rate at later times with cemented monoblock procedures was surprising. Together with the fact that implant survival from the AOA NJRR is increased for cemented implants than for uncemented implants into the medium term (AOA 2009) and the existence of previous work demonstrating improved functional outcome and pain scores with cemented implants (Khan et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0020" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002a</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0021" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>b</a></span>, Parker and Gurusamy <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0029" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span>), it is becoming increasingly difficult to justify the continued preference of some surgeons for uncemented implants.</p>
 <p>The strength of this study lies in the large numbers that were available for analysis. Due to high data completion rates and stringent data validation protocols by the AOA NJRR, the data are robust and easily lend themselves to this type of analysis.</p>
 <p>There are many possible explanations for our findings. Firstly, a weakness of this study is that selection of implant fixation was not randomized. In that regard, other patient factors may have influenced the surgeon's decision to avoid cement, which may not have been adequately adjusted for in our analysis. For example, it has been shown that pre-existing cardiac disease is an independent risk factor for cement-related mortality (Parvizi et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0032" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span>). Other risk factors for increased perioperative mortality with hip fracture include age, sex, and comorbidities (Hannan et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0015" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2001</a></span>, Jiang et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0017" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005</a></span>, Vestergaard et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0042" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2007b</a></span>, Varez-Nebreda et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0040" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2008</a></span>). The Australian Orthopaedic Association National Joint Replacement Registry does not collect comorbidity data, and in that regard we could not rule out the possibility that selection bias for fixation method influenced overall patient mortality. Our subcategorization of procedures into modular and monoblock components was an effort to control for this variable with a surrogate measure, as monoblock components are typically used in the frail elderly for quicker surgery and as there is less functional demand on the component postoperatively.</p>
 <p>In a separate analysis, we found that there was a favorable survival rate at 1 year in patients aged 71–80 and &gt; 80 when cemented monoblock implants were used rather than uncemented monoblock components. The reasons for this are unclear, but they may relate to selection of fixation method based on patient variables not captured by the AOA NJRR. For example, it is possible that elderly patients receiving cemented monoblock components are generally in better health than those treated with uncemented monoblock hemiarthroplasty, and are felt to be less likely to succumb to cement-related drop in systolic blood pressure intraoperatively. Alternatively, the opposite may be true—as for hip prostheses, generally fitter (younger, healthier) individuals receive cementless prostheses. There is also considerable state-to-state variability in preference for fixation method (AOA 2009), and individual hospital trends, which probably reflect different training and philosophies across the country. Further subanalysis of the relationship of these variables makes broad conclusions difficult, as patient numbers decrease with further subcategorization.</p>
 <p>The cause of death was also not investigated in this study; thus, we could not directly link mortality to surgery-related issues. Certainly, 1-day and 1-week mortality are likely to be associated with perioperative factors. Consistent with our study, Foster et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0013" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005</a></span>) found a higher 30-day mortality rate in uncemented (9%) vs. cemented (1%) hemiarthroplasties in a retrospective review of 244 patients, despite similar ASA grades in both groups.</p>
 <p>Parker and Gurusamy (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0029" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span>) published a meta-analysis on the outcome of cemented hip arthroplasties vs. uncemented components for hip fracture, and found that mobility and pain at 1 year postoperatively was better in the cemented group. There was no difference in perioperative mortality in their analysis. This report included over 1,900 patients, although still substantially smaller than our study. The same findings were corroborated in a separate meta-analysis of 18 publications comparing cemented and uncemented arthroplasty for hip fractures (Khan et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0021" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002b</a></span>). Khan's group further compared 121 uncemented to 123 cemented Austin-Moore hemiarthroplasty patients done in 2 hospitals (Khan et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0020" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002a</a></span>). Patients treated with uncemented Austin-Moore implants had more pain, worse function in terms of walking and dependence on walking aids, and reduced capacity to perform activities of daily living compared to patients with cement fixation. There was no statistically significant difference in mortality or non-fatal medical complication rates related to type of fixation used. There were more intraoperative fractures (3/121 uncemented vs. 0/123 cemented), more dislocations (3/121 vs. 0/123), and a higher failure rate (numbers not reported) in patients with uncemented implants. In a single-institution audit, Singh and Deshmukh (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0039" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>) reported a higher overall reoperation and revision rate using uncemented Austin-Moore implants than when using cemented Thompson hemiarthroplasties. Patients treated with cemented implants also had a higher overall satisfaction rate relative to those with the uncemented stem. In a small, randomized study comparing cemented Thompson implants to uncemented Austin-Moore implants (Emery et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1991</a></span>), pain and dependence on walking aids was less if the femoral component was fixed with cement. There was no difference in mortality or perioperative complications in either group. In a retrospective review of 107 patients treated with Thompson hemiarthroplasty for displaced femoral neck fractures, Sikorski and Millar (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0038" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1977</a></span>) failed to demonstrate any increased rate of mortality, myocardial infarction, cerebrovascular incident, cardiac failure, or postoperative hypotension whether or not cement was used. Similar findings have been reported with other comparisons of cemented and uncemented implants (Lausten and Vedel <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0024" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1982</a></span>, Lo et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0026" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1994</a></span>).</p>
 <p>Although pulmonary fat embolization is much less common with uncemented components, embolic events do occur (Orsini et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0028" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1987</a></span>, Ries et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0036" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1993</a></span>, Pitto et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0034" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1999</a></span>), and this is probably related to increased intramedullary pressures during instrumentation of the femoral canal (Kallos et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0018" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1974</a></span>, Orsini et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0028" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1987</a></span>). Wenda et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0044" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1995</a></span>) showed that reaming of the intramedullary canal produces pressures averaging 835 mmHg, and that only 200 mmHg is required for fat intravasation and embolization. This compares with maximum pressures of approximately 846 mmHg, demonstrated with introduction of cement into the femoral canal in a dog model by Orsini et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0028" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1987</a></span>). In fact, there have been a few case reports outlining perioperative fat embolism syndrome and mortality due to fat embolization with uncemented hip arthroplasty (Arroyo et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0002" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1994</a></span>, Gelinas et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0014" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2000</a></span>). It is also known that intraoperative complications are higher with uncemented hemiarthroplasty, including iatrogenic femoral fracture (Foster et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0013" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005</a></span>, Weinrauch et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0043" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>). A randomized, controlled trial investigating the prevalence of fat and bone marrow emboli in the lung based on right-atrium blood sampling showed similar prevalences with cemented and uncemented components (Kim et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0022" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002</a></span>). Furthermore, it has been shown that proper femoral canal lavage and vacuum suction reduce embolic events with cement implantation (Christie et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1995</a></span>, Pitto et al. <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0033" data-reflink="" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1998</a></span>). Modern cement techniques may therefore account for the lower incidence of perioperative mortality with use of cement compared to earlier studies.</p>
 <p>In conclusion, this study shows a small but statistically significantly increased risk of mortality at 1 day when cement is used for monoblock hemiarthroplasty procedures. By 1 week, there is no longer a mortality advantage to avoiding cement, and by 1 year, mortality is less when cement is used. This may be due to a higher overall revision rate with uncemented monoblock components. When modular components are compared, there is no difference in mortality at any time analyzed, although there is a higher implant revision rate when uncemented components are used. These data support the use of cemented hemiarthroplasty components in patients with hip fracture.</p>
