<div id="S001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec-type_intro NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i3" class="section-heading-2">1. Introduction</h2>
 <p>Boosted by the COVID-19 pandemic, the fashion industry has been forced to speed up its digital transformation, bringing traditional offline services online. In the UK, 77% of retail businesses are employing AI in e-commerce through the use of chatbots (Statista, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0047" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>).</p>
 <p>Studies involving AI and fashion as a discipline have looked at factors that determine consumer acceptance (Rese, Ganster, &amp; Baier, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0042" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>; Sanny, Susastra, Roberts, &amp; Yusramdaleni, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0044" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), forecasting and production management (Sun &amp; Zhang, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0048" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>), smart textiles (Roh, Chi, &amp; Kang, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0043" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2010</a></span>), trend analysis of colour palettes (Lai &amp; Westland, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0020" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), accuracy of image labelling through machine learning (ML) (Park &amp; Choi, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0037" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) and ‘conversational agents’, also known as ‘chatbots’ (Grewal, Roggeveen, &amp; Nordfält, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0016" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2017</a></span>).</p>
 <p>Chatbots can provide personalised shopping experiences across physical and online channels (Grewal et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0016" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2017</a></span>) and promote consumer wellbeing (Skjuve, Følstad, Fostervold, &amp; Brandtzaeg, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0046" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2021</a></span>). However, the main challenge remains on the complexity of human language and the chatbot’s effectiveness within this context (Pantano &amp; Pizzi, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0035" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>).</p>
 <p>Chatbots may be viewed as Natural User Interfaces (Tan &amp; Liew, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0049" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) for users to interact with a computer more intuitively through natural language, easing acceptance of e-commerce, especially for fashion items. Thus, chatbots may be seen as an essential, relevant interface element for many fashion e-commerce tasks such as providing recommendations, exploring and searching huge catalogues, complementing virtual fitting room’s features, and delivering (post-sale) customer services (<a href="#F0001">Figure 1</a>).</p>

 <p></p>
 <p>When it comes to designing and adopting chatbots within the consumer journey, Pantano and Pizzi (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0035" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) inform that: retailers might want to choose already designed chatbot platforms or to design their own; from a technological point of view, conversational agents are designed to mimic natural language; however, analytical skills to learn from consumer data are still required; and, social media (e.g. instant messengers such as Facebook Messenger) and voice-based AI (utilising audio recognition) such as Siri and Alexa, are the most used platforms. From a computational perspective, there is a trend to improve the responsiveness of the chatbot and patents were mostly designed to provide new algorithms related to Natural Language Processing (NLP), digital communication and consumer management.</p>
 <p>Since retailers are willing to opt for designing their own chatbot, it is crucial that managers and designers understand the current technological barriers and opportunities. Given this context, the aim of this paper is to provide design and research opportunities for future fashion chatbots applications by answering the following research questions:</p>
 <ul class="NLM_list NLM_list-list_type-bullet">
  <li><p class="inline">RQ1: Which are the current proposed chatbots design approaches for e-commerce in general, and particularly for fashion applications?</p></li>
  <li><p class="inline">RQ2: How could research on e-commerce chatbots be categorised in an integrated manner?</p></li>
  <li><p class="inline">RQ3: What are the research opportunities of chatbots design to deal with the specificities of fashion e-commerce applications?</p></li>
 </ul>
 <p></p>
 <p>The main contributions of this study are: (i) the proposal of an interdisciplinary categorisation with an integrated view of the theme of ‘chatbots in fashion e-commerce’ that can serve as support for guiding professionals and researchers, from academy to industry, interested in understanding such kind of agents; (ii) the provision of a synthesised basis for future reference and research on the theme.</p>

 <h2 id="_i5" class="section-heading-2">2. Methodology and research design</h2>
 <p>The theme-based literature review was carried out according to a 3-phase methodology we interactively adjusted from that by Okoli (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0032" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2015</a></span>) for simplicity and fidelity to actual procedures for the task at hand. Phases are not necessarily sequential: one may return to a previous phase after (partial) execution of a next phase – e.g. the review results provided a base to categorise chatbot studies and to enhance details of the review’s design and execution and associated analysis (see <a href="#F0002">Figure 2</a>).</p>

 <div id="S002-S2001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i7">2.1. Search and selection strategies</h3>
  <p>To answer the RQs, the search string</p>
  <p><i>(Chatbot OR Dialog System OR Conversational Agent OR Virtual Assistant OR Digital Assistant)</i></p>
  <p><i>AND</i></p>
  <p><i>((E-comm* OR Ecomm* OR Retail) <u class="uu">OR</u> (Fashion OR Garment OR Apparel OR Clothing OR Cosmetics OR Makeup OR Make-up))</i></p>
  <p>was conducted to collect papers published from 2011 to 2021 on the theme of interest from the following databases: ACM Digital Library, Emerald, Google Scholar, IEEE Xplore Digital Library, Mendeley (Elsevier), Microsoft Academic, SagePub, ScienceDirect, Scopus, Springer, Taylor and Francis Online, IBM’s TechDocs, and Wiley Online Library. This covers 10 years of research in this field (preliminary searches on some of the databases revealed no study on the theme prior to 2014 – see <a href="#F0006">Figure 6</a>). To avoid limitations, we adopted a second search string that is not restricted to ‘e-commerce’ AND ‘fashion’ only. Variations for ‘fashion’ were also included such as apparel, garment, clothing, cosmetics, and makeup. As for ‘chatbots’, we utilised dialog system, conversational agent, virtual assistant and digital assistant as variations. A study was excluded if it was: i) written in a language other than English; ii) not available online for download; and iii) addressing the theme of chatbots but not for digital retail purposes. Three types of work were included: i) journal papers, ii) conference papers, and iii) book chapters.</p>
 </div>

  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i8">2.2. Categorisation</h3>

  <p></p>
  <p>Chatbots as conversational recommender systems take a more sophisticated approach, offering a richer set of interactions that help improve preference elicitation and interact with users through natural language (NL). As such, chatbots can provide mechanisms to capture contextual information, as what has been intended by the so-called context-aware recommender systems. In the case of chatbots in fashion e-commerce, other features related to online sales could be required - e,g,, those that engage and persuade customers to buy a product, eventually involving negotiation dialogues, playing the role of a salesperson conversing with a customer (Jusoh, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0018" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>). This kind of system deals with the main challenges of natural-language-based interaction, such as NL understanding, information extraction, and NL generation, which can be specific for the fashion domain.</p>

<div id="S003" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i10" class="section-heading-2">3. Results and discussion</h2>
 <p>After applying the proposed methodology, 76 out of 5959 papers were selected: 46 describing research on chatbot computational aspects; and 30 being surveys about the state of the art or the user experience using chatbots. Firstly, it is worth noting the growth of research on the theme of chatbots for (fashion) e-commerce since 2014 (see <a href="#F0004">Figure 4</a>). Such increasing interest may be explained by the advent of DL and the subsequent interest to comprehend non-computational aspects of chatbots.</p>

<div id="S004" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i12" class="section-heading-2">3.1. Computational studies</h2>
 <div id="S004-S2001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <div id="S004-S2001-S3001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_3">
   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i13">3.1.1. Domain, language and implementation details</h4>
   <p>Most research on chatbot computational aspects had English as their primary language (76.3%), followed by papers on Indonesian chatbots (6.8%) and other languages like Chinese and Bangla. However, the resulting papers were mostly not fashion-specific (87.7%). Contrastingly, a few papers like Liao, Zhou, Ma, Hong, and Chua (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0023" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) and Vaccaro, Agarwalla, Shivakumar, and Kumar (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0050" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) were fashion-specific. It is also worth mentioning that, while non-computational research mainly employed a diversity of ready-to-use chatbot tools like Amazon Alexa, computational papers usually focus on chatbot development using a specific programming language.</p>
  </div>
  <div id="S004-S2001-S3002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_3">
   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i14">3.1.2. Media</h4>
   <p>Most recent papers already consider natural language text as the input method (79.4%), except for Aarthi (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0002" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), Pricilla, Lestari, and Dharma (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0040" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) and Wintersberger, Klotz, and Riener (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0051" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) which allow the user to choose from a fixed list of options in specific situations. A few studies (15.1%) are voice-based, and others employ avatars to enhance users’ confidence and perception of the system (Eisman, Navarro, &amp; Castro, 2016).</p>
  </div>
  <div id="S004-S2001-S3003" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_3">
   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i15">3.1.3. Dialog control</h4>
   <p>A turn is each single contribution that composes a dialog (Jurafsky &amp; Martin, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0017" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>). Reactive agents respond to user interactions, thus solely exploiting information explicitly provided by the user’s initiative. Conversely, proactive chatbots employ an engagement strategy to interact and influence users, making use of predictions about users’ demands.</p>
   <p>Employed dialog turns were mostly reactive, except for Aarthi (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0002" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) and Liu, Jiang, Xiong, Yang, and Ye (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0024" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) systems, which could take control of the dialog flow. The dialog control options in <button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="T0001">Table 1</button> were well-explored. Neural-based controls seem to be a trending research direction. And, the majority are made to solve a specific task, such as answering questions about product characteristics or mediating a purchase. Most papers aimed at solving retail tasks with a minority addressing ‘chit-chat’ chatbots. This aspect could be potentially explored in fashion-specific chatbot research.</p>
   <p>To aid users in solving a specific task, chatbots must have a dialog policy, which is responsible for deciding which action the system should take in the next iteration. Although many distinct approaches have been proposed, we identified three main subcategories in the literature:</p>
   <ol class="NLM_list NLM_list-list_type-order">
    <li><p class="inline">Predict the user’s intent, and then choose a specific answer in a finite set of predefined responses;</p></li>
    <li><p class="inline">Analyse the similarity between the user’s questions and questions in a dataset, and choose an answer accordingly; and</p></li>
    <li><p class="inline">Sequence-to-sequence DL approaches decode the user intent first, and synthesise a response.</p></li>
   </ol>
   <p></p>
  </div>
  <div id="S004-S2001-S3004" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_3">
   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i16">3.1.4. Input processing and natural language understanding</h4>

Interest in DL picked up starting in 2017. Nevertheless, classical ML approaches and rule-based methods are still employed in state-of-the-art research.</p>

   <p>When user interactions are enacted through buttons or multiple-choice interfaces, the expressed intents and/or other relevant information may be straightforwardly comprehended by the chatbot. However, when human natural language contained in unstructured data is allowed in the form of text (possibly extracted from speech), specific algorithms for Natural Language Understanding (NLU) must be employed. Rule-based algorithms employ handcrafted hard rules by using, for instance, the Artificial Intelligence Mark-up Language (AIML). Classical ML algorithms in general combine the extraction of handcrafted features from unstructured textual data, such as n-gram counts or the Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) statistical measure. DL algorithms present state-of-the-art performance by employing deep neural network architectures for sequence processing, which mainly include variations of CNN and RNN to recognise complex patterns from data.</p>
   <p>Chatbot answers also follow approaches that resemble NLU algorithms, and thus may be categorised as rule-based, when sentences are generated from pre-written templates; or neural-based, when sentences are generated from textual training data. In the latter, variants of CNN, RNN, and Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) are among the most popular approaches. For a comprehensive introduction to NLP algorithms, we refer the reader to Jurafsky and Martin (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0017" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>).</p>

   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i21">3.1.6. Validation</h4>
   <p>Regarding experiments to measure chatbots’ sentence generation effectiveness, the literature brings mainly two approaches: similarity metrics to compare a chatbot’s answer to that of a human, and/or expert evaluation (human judgment). To quantify the quality of a sentence generated by chatbots, Nie, Wang, Hong, Wang, and Tian (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0031" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) and Gao et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0014" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) applied the BLEU Score (Papineni, Roukos, Ward, &amp; Zhu, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0036" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002</a></span>), a metric for evaluating similarity in automatic language translations based on the word's position in a sentence. Similarly, Chen, Li, Ji, Zhou, and Chen (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) proposed to use the Distinct-1/2 score (Li, Galley, Brockett, Gao, &amp; Dolan, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0021" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2016</a></span>) which is based on the ratios of distinct unigrams and bigrams.</p>
   <p>Nevertheless, similarity metrics do not consider syntax and semantics aspects. Therefore, another chatbot validation approach is through the assistance of linguistic (human) experts to evaluate the meaning of the chatbot's sentences. In Nie et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0031" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>), Chen et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) and Qiu et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0041" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2017</a></span>), experts judged whether a response fits the question context considering fluency, relevance, informativeness, semantic consistency, and syntax precision. Considering the entire process, Nazir, Khan, Ahmed, Jami, &amp; Wasi, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0030" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span> followed Turing’s Loebner principle (Mauldin, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0026" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1994</a></span>), where the experts interact with the chatbot for ten minutes to evaluate the chatbot based on context orientation, dynamism, and grammar structure.</p>
  </div>
  <div id="S004-S2001-S3007" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_3">
   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i22">3.1.7. Fashion domain-specific results</h4>
   <p>Multimodal chatbots have been proposed to integrate visual media with textual chatbot interfaces. Liao et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0023" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) proposed a multimodal domain knowledge enriched fashion chatbot that understands the semantics of product image and modifies the attributes during back-end retrieval, offers matching suggestions, and generates responses with different modalities. De Carolis, De Gemmis, and Lops (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2015</a></span>), (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2017</a></span>) exploit multimodality to consider extra-rational consumer factors such as attitudes, emotions, likes and dislikes, to provide refined recommendations accordingly.</p>
   <p>Virtual assistants can also be made to mimic a salesperson (Morotti, Donatiello, &amp; Marfia, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0029" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>). Sapna et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0045" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) researched chatbots and ML methods to replicate the offline shopping experience.</p>
   <p>Fashion specific knowledge is another non-general chatbot aspect for future research to close a large existing gap. The ontology-driven chatbot model based on the semantic web of Nazir et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0030" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) illustrates results that can be expected.</p>
  </div>
 </div>
 <div id="S004-S2002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i23">3.2. Non-computational studies</h3>
  <p>Non-computational studies comprised 20 categories, including consumer trust, acceptance, satisfaction and experience. We also identified that chatbot acceptance has been assessed through models such as the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and/or the Use &amp; Gratification Theory (U&amp;G), mainly focusing on perceived usefulness, ease of use, and enjoyment (Kasilingam, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0019" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>; Rese et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0042" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>). Besides enjoyment and utilitarian factors (Rese et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0042" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), chatbot acceptance also relies on social presence common in human-human interactions (Chaves &amp; Gerosa, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0004" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>). Chatbot acceptance is also influenced by privacy concerns and might also vary across different demographics (Rese et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0042" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>). Anthropomorphism (Go &amp; Sundar, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0015" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) and privacy (Cheng &amp; Jiang, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) were two factors that emerged from the review that show an impact on chatbot trustworthiness.</p>
  <div id="S004-S2002-S3001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_3">
   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i24">3.2.1. Fashion domain-specific results</h4>
   <p>To circumvent scarcity of training datasets for the fashion-domain, Sapna et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0045" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) combined a Recommender System (RS) to a Fashion-Knowledgeable Component (FKC) into a chatbot (‘Athena’). Athena’s RS uses the product inventory of the e-commerce site while its FKC uses fashion information collected from social media, models’ photographs and stylists’ curation of fashion items. Athena’s recommendations are made from inventory-style associations and are thus, impersonal. The fashion database needs to be (frequently) aligned to inventory, however.</p>
   <p>We have identified a trend towards the use of chatbots as co-creators of value during purchase through different roles specific to fashion (e.g. salesperson or stylist). For example, in-person fashion stylists were implemented to elicit chatbot requirements that facilitate building trust and dealing with uncertainty in e-commerce (Vaccaro et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0050" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>). Rese et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0042" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) have also found consumer gender differences in fashion e-commerce, indicating a higher use of chatbots by females.</p>
  </div>
 </div>
</div>
<div id="S005" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i25" class="section-heading-2">4. Research directions</h2>
 <p>The main research gaps and needs the literature review unearthed and that serve to base suggestions of chatbot research directions in general and for the fashion domain in particular are presented here along the computational and non-computational perspectives</p>
 <div id="S005-S2001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i26">4.1. Computational perspective</h3>
  <p>Research for innovatively applying chatbots to e-commerce settings in general will move forward in a three-pronged way:</p>
  <ol class="NLM_list NLM_list-list_type-order">
   <li><p class="inline"><i>Evolution of DL approaches for sales assistance</i>: as revealed in <a href="#F0005">Figure 5</a>, DL approaches have emerged and become the trending research direction for chatbot tasks related to NLP;</p></li>
   <li><p class="inline"><i>Availability of databases to enhance DL training</i>: DL algorithms strongly rely on huge datasets for training. Our literature review points to scarcity of public datasets for chatbots training. Alternatively, Li, Li, and Ji (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0022" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2021</a></span>) used a dataset of conversations between customers and customer service; Chen et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>), a dataset with user reviews from e-commerce; and Yu et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0053" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2017</a></span>), a generic question pair dataset. The publication of such datasets, for several domains not just fashion, is a promising research direction, including how to deal with data privacy; and,</p></li>
   <li><p class="inline"><i>Investigation of audio-enabled chatbots in Natural User Interfaces (NUIs):</i> using audio cues to evaluate user experience of NUIs is still limited. Prajwal, Mamatha, Ravi, Manoj, and Joisa (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0039" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) and Palma, Seeger, and Heinzl (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0033" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) consider voice-only chatbots; and De Carolis et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2015</a></span>), Eisman et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0013" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2016</a></span>) and Tan and Liew (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0049" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) besides voice, have an avatar. Most of these studies convert speech to text for the chatbot, except for De Carolis et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2015</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2017</a></span>) that uses audio input directly to enable recognition of voice intonation and prosody to better determine users’ intent by reducing loss of information on cues due to voice-text conversion.</p></li>
  </ol>
  <p></p>
  <div id="S005-S2001-S3001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_3">
   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i27">4.1.1. Fashion-domain specific computational research opportunities</h4>
   <p>Four major opportunities may be highlighted:</p>
   <ol class="NLM_list NLM_list-list_type-order">
    <li><p class="inline">More encompassing, real-life, professional-grade datasets on fashion items are needed if fashion chatbots are to be more extensively trained and trusted: given that no large public dataset is readily available, Nazir et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0030" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) alternatively collected data manually from clothing brands websites and from Facebook posts and comments. Others like Liao et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0023" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) proposed to transfer knowledge from richer domains.</p></li>
    <li><p class="inline">Applying chatbots to fashion e-commerce specific needs such as multimedia conversations (e.g. text, voice and images): Pantano, Passavanti, Priporas, and Verteramo (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0034" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) also revealed a lack of innovative technologies in the fashion luxury industry. De Carolis et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2015</a></span>) recommended new clothes based on users’ visual cues, Sapna et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0045" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) make the chatbot ask users their preferences and Liao et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0023" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) proposed a multimodal chatbot to gather user’s visual and textual clothing needs. Future work could explore chatbot’s retrieval of users’ short- and long-term preferences to better recommend fashion products.</p></li>
    <li><p class="inline">Integration of chatbots to other fashion applications in different points within the consumer journey: since chatbots can provide personalised information for consumers across their journey (Vaccaro et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0050" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>), research that explores such integration, e.g. virtually trying a recommended item on, shows potential. Another opportunity is the integration with Augmented Reality (AR) – e.g. in Virtual Fitting Room (VFR) applications. Moriuchi, Landers, Colton, and Hair (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0028" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) compare e-commerce apps that use chatbots and those which use AR, but they do not propose integration of both to serve the fashion domain.</p></li>
   </ol>
   <p></p>
  </div>
 </div>
 <div id="S005-S2002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i28">4.2. Non-computational perspective</h3>
  <p>Future research on non-computational aspects of chatbots for e-commerce that can be applied in the fashion domain appears more promising in the following 4 areas:</p>
  <ol class="NLM_list NLM_list-list_type-order">
   <li><p class="inline"><i>Chatbot acceptance and design for different demographics</i>: Rese et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0042" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) found that females had a negative attitude towards chatbots due to the technology immaturity and privacy issues. Models that measure consumer acceptance should be addressed (e.g. TAM and U&amp;G, mentioned earlier) in future research for chatbot contexts, particularly when considering differences in Womenswear and Menswear categories.</p></li>
   <li><p class="inline"><i>Consumer autonomy and identity in chatbot consumer experience:</i>consumer autonomy (Ameen, Hosany, &amp; Tarhini, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0003" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2021</a></span>) is related to the perceived sense of control that consumers have over the interaction with chatbots and it can be attached to motivational factors (e.g. Self-Determination Theory). Future studies might address the role that consumer autonomy and identity play in consumer trust and acceptance, for example, by measuring chatbots’ design approaches that can trigger these states.</p></li>
   <li><p class="inline"><i>Chatbot design, consumer trust and privacy</i>: since consumers might see chatbots with negative eyes if there are privacy concerns, consumer trust is another factor that can be explored further. Aspects such as transparent advice (Vaccaro et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0050" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) and problem-solving (Chung, Ko, Joung, &amp; Kim, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0007" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), could be investigated in future research, addressing the role that design plays in this context and if other factors (e.g. social elements, cultural values, self-identity) influence consumers’ trust on chatbots.</p></li>
   <li><p class="inline"><i>Perceived enjoyment, usability, and usefulness:</i> perceived enjoyment and the utilitarian nature of the interaction (e.g. whether useful or not) also influence consumer acceptance. User experience and usability were also factors highlighted by this review. Further studies that analyse the influence of these elements in chatbot design for e-commerce are needed. Other aspects that might be added into this area is the influence of playfulness and gamification in perceived enjoyment of chatbots.</p></li>
  </ol>
  <p></p>
  <div id="S005-S2002-S3001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_3">
   <h4 class="section-heading-4" id="_i29">4.2.1. Fashion-domain specific non-computational research opportunities</h4>
   <p>Promising fashion chatbot research that is essentially non-computational and that can be inferred from the review’s results:</p>
   <ol class="NLM_list NLM_list-list_type-order">
    <li><p class="inline"><i>Culture and gender-aware chatbots</i>: fashion retail differs across cultures and individual fit (womenswear vs. menswear), chatbots need to be tailored accordingly. Inclusive studies such as cultural and gender-related research in this field are in order.</p></li>
    <li><p class="inline"><i>Multiuser chatting</i>: Merrilees and Miller (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0027" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>) observed that traditional shopping with a companion influences the consumer experience. Alone consumers tend to be more price sensitive. Future studies may explore the way fashion consumers seek for advice from chatbots that could be experimented with by adjusting social factors (e.g. including a friend in the conversation), evaluating the impact of these factors on user acceptance levels, and bring said factors into the dialog when indicators (rejected number of recommendations, say) cross thresholds.</p></li>
    <li><p class="inline"><i>Designbots</i>: Fashion chatbots may be made to behave as a fashion designer, providing a platform to support co-creation of value (Colombi, Kim, &amp; Wyatt, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0008" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>). Conversational platforms can provide insights for brands to recognise consumer value (Copulsky, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0009" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>), which means that future research in this area can also enhance the consumer experience.</p></li>
    <li><p class="inline"><i>Persuasion</i>: e-commerce chatbots should be able to function as a persuasive salesperson (Jusoh, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0018" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>). Building persuasion capabilities into fashion chatbots, given the scarcity of real-life training datasets, will require research to elicit expertise and tactics from fashion offline stylists and sales experts.</p></li>
    <li><p class="inline"><i>Replicating other offline fashion experience</i>s: Research that mines insights from consumer-stylists’ conversations, similarly to the work by Vaccaro et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0050" data-reflink="_i32 _i33 _i34" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>), could be also useful to other parts of the fashion supply chain - e.g. design and marketing of new fashion collections.</p></li>
   </ol>
   <p></p>
  </div>

<div id="S006" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i30" class="section-heading-2">5. Conclusion</h2>
 <p>This paper presented an interdisciplinary, comprehensive review of what has been done in the field of conversational agents for fashion and retail e-commerce. Thus, this study adds to specialised literature in the field of chatbot design for fashion e-commerce by providing a comprehensive map of chatbot approaches that can be deployed by retailers. This study also revealed that despite the increasing investment in chatbots for e-commerce in general, research and use in fashion e-commerce are still relatively modest. Thus, further research and development on this topic are still needed.</p>
