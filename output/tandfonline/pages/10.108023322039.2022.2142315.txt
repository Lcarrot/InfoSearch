<div id="s0001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec-type_intro NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i3" class="section-heading-2">1. Introduction</h2>
 <p>The banking industry is the main guarantor of financial system stability in any country and specifically in developing countries as it is the main channel of funds redistribution and the backbone of the economy (Jreisat et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0052" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2021</a></span>; Karim et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0060" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2021</a></span>; Sun et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0096" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>). Hence, ensuring efficiency within these banks is crucial to avoid shocks to the financial industry and maintain economic growth (Aghimien et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0001" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2016</a></span>). In fact, banks are the circuit used to execute monetary policy, allocate credit, transfer wealth, and act even as providers of payment services. Bank management is in charge of monitoring efficiency levels by measuring performance, this can be defined as the evaluation of various financial indicators comparing the results with the target set by management, previous performance, and peer group (Rabbani et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0088" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2022</a></span>). Being able to measure efficiency levels in banks determines how successful a bank is in managing its operations and achieving its goals. In the extant literature, several models were suggested and applied to measure efficiency, some of them adopted the parametric approach, the non-parametric approaches, or even used mixed approaches.</p>
 <p>Oil price volatility is believed to have an impact on bank efficiency in a given country and as a consequence on economic growth. In fact, increasing volatility in oil price is negatively related to economic growth since such increases generate higher inflation rates which is undesired to any economy (Awerbuch &amp; Raphael, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0006" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>). Moreover, crude oil volatility has a negative impact on exchange rate, noting that inflation, exchange rate, and many other factors are major risks to be mitigated by banks on a continuous basis to ensure bank efficiency (Ojebiyi &amp; Wilson). In view of the above, policy makers keep a very close eye on Oil price volatility and its impact on performance within banks involved in countries that export oil (Poghosyan &amp; Hesse, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0087" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2009</a></span>). This topic has been researched by many scholars and practitioners for the importance of the impact Oil Price changes have on efficiency; however, despite the numerous papers investigating the factors affecting efficiency in banks, many gaps are still prevailing and are presented in the literature review.</p>
 <p>This study focuses mainly on the gap revolving around Oil price volatility and banking efficiency, there are few studies highlighting the similar issues such as the impact of oil crisis on the banking performance. Hawaldar et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0040" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2017</a></span>) highlighted the impact of the oil crisis on the banking performance in the Kingdom of Bahrain. Whereas Abdelaziz, et al., (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0090" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), examined the oil and gas price shocks on the bank performance. Robert and Andre (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0062" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) examined the performance of Canadian banks and oil price movements. These studies mainly focus country &amp; bank-specific variables. Our study is using cross-country panel data as a tool to illustrate the link between banking efficiency and oil price using a sample data of 112 banks, over the time interval 2003 to 2018 to inspect banking sector relative efficiency following a non-parametric methodology known as Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA). The paper applies a two-stage model to process the empirical results consisting in using the Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) to identify the scores of banks efficiency at a first stage (Stage 1) and determining how volatility in Oil price has impact on these scores of efficiencies on a second stage (Stage 2).</p>
 <p>While many studies inspected the impact of oil prices as an independent variable on many financial offerings and primary economic determinants; only two studies (outside the scope of Islamic banking) provided empirical evidence on the relationship between OPV-BE. Poghosyan and Hesse (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0087" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2009</a></span>) precisely inspected Oil Price effect on bank profitability using efficiency measurements to assess profitability levels within the bank studied sample. The second paper published by Kaffash et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0054" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) applied the DEA method to determine the effect of OPV on BE from 2001 to 2011 using unbalanced panel data from banks in Middle East oil exporting countries (MEOE) where macroeconomic aspects were heavily impacted by OPV.</p>
 <p>This paper comes as an extension to previous few research adopting the same data selection technique in terms of variables to be taken into consideration over the time interval 2003–2018 for BRICS countries setting the OPV (Oil Price Volatility) as the independent variable BE (Bank Efficiency) as the dependent variable in order to provide empirical evidence on the effect of Oil price volatility on banking efficiency.</p>
 <p>There are few studies highlighting the market reactions to oil prices changing dramatically with the level of prices and potential structural changes in oil prices using different methodologies (Baumeister &amp; Kilian, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0009" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2016</a></span>; Chen et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0019" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2015</a></span>; Narayan &amp; Narayan, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0077" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2014</a></span>). Present study differs from the above studies in several ways and offers significant contribution to the existing strand of literature. The main contribution of the study is adding a brick to the existing scarce literature of the relationship between OPV on BE by deploying the DEA method in BRICS countries noting that existing studies did not investigate the topic within this region or provided numerical evidence on the level of impact of oil price volatility on Banks efficiency. In fact, a study done by Wanke et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0101" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) measured banks’ efficiencies in BRICS countries without linking it to oil price volatility or taken it as the independent factor.</p>
 <p>The aim behind focusing on BRICS countries in this paper can be summarized as follows: (1) Banks plays a very important intermediation role in reallocating capital provided by Oil fund transfers. (2) OPV may substantially affect the availability of funds of multiple industries in BRICS countries and their performance. (3) Economies in BRICS countries depend heavily on oil price yields. 4) Up to the authors knowledge, no study had measured the consequence of OPV on BE on BRICS countries and this study is the first study to shed the light on this issue.</p>
 <p>In addition, BRICS Countries worth to be investigated and examined which is that the BRICS countries have a population of more than 3 billion, which is greater than the combined population of the United States and Canada. This is a population that accounts for 42% of the world’s population. An untapped market of this size could be the most valuable hedge against globalization in the long run. They are all members of the G20, and their collective nominal GDP of USD 16 trillion represents 1/5 of the world’s gross domestic product. Overall, BRICS, however, is outperformed by the G7, and each of the BRICS nations has significantly different economic indicators and other indices. see <a href="#f0001">figure 1</a>.</p>

 <h2 id="_i5" class="section-heading-2">2. Review of literature</h2>
 <p>The literature review is presented in two sections: (1) Bank efficiency denoted (BE) and its impact on performance within banks. (2) Oil Price Volatility denoted (OPV) and its importance as economic indicator moving other markets and its correlation with financial stability and economic growth.</p>
 <div id="s0002-s2001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i6">2.1. Bank efficiency and performance</h3>
  <p>Many researchers have explored the importance of bank efficiency basing their studies on the most two commonly used approaches in banks efficiency measurement that are the DEA—a non-parametric approach and stochastic frontier analysis (SFA)—a parametric approach (Banker et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0008" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1984</a></span>; Charnes et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0017" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1978</a></span>; Kamarudin et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0058" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2016</a></span>). As for the area of focus of our study which is the BRICS region, main existing researches adopted the non-parametric technique with no defined data distribution due to the possibility of having many output factors and due to the fact that the Decision making unit in a bank denoted (DMU) depends on the ability of converting inputs into outputs (Alam et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0004" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2021</a></span>; Banker et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0007" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2010</a></span>; Drake et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0025" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>; Havrylchyk, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0039" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>; Hussain et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0044" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>; Isik &amp; Hassan, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0045" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003a</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0046" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003b</a></span>; Jreisat, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0049" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>; Jreisat &amp; Paul, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0051" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2010</a></span>; Kao &amp; Liu, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0059" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2014</a></span>; Paradi et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0083" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2011</a></span>; Wanke et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0101" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>).</p>
  <p>Other studies have adopted cross-country investigations or even mixed methodology techniques using both parametric and non-parametric methods through different stages of their research (Beccalli, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0010" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2007</a></span>; Bonin et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0012" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005a</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0013" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005b</a></span>; Casu &amp; Girardone, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0014" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2009</a></span>; Casu &amp; Molyneux, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0015" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003</a></span>; Chortareas et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0020" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2011</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0021" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2012</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0022" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2013</a></span>; Kamarudin et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0057" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2013</a></span>; Pastor, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0084" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002</a></span>; Weill, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0102" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span>).</p>
  <p>Lampe and Hilgers (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0067" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2015</a></span>) for example conducted a document co-citation analysis and discovered that Stochastic Frontier Analysis (SFA) and DEA were mostly applied to explore the banking domain. Meanwhile, Emrouznejad and Yang (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0027" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2018</a></span>) claimed that DEA was the second most applied method for 2015–2016 in the banking segment. Kaffash and Marra (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0055" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2016</a></span>) reviewed the DEA techniques within financial services boundary. Essentially, this non-parametric method of DEA generates piece-wise surface of decision-making units (DMU) with the best performance, while distance from surface is measured to determine efficiency of other units (Charnes et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0017" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1978</a></span>). The SFA (parametric method; Aigner et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0002" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1977</a></span>; Meeusen &amp; Den Broeck, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0073" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1977</a></span>), with a starting point in stochastic production frontier models, presumes deviations from frontier, such as random glitches and inefficiency (Akhigbe &amp; McNulty, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0003" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003</a></span>).</p>
  <p>Kaffash et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0054" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>) analyzed 50 studies published from 1999 to November 2016 in order to assess the effect of environmental variables on BE based on SFA and DEA models. Main outcomes of their studies can be summarized as follows: (1) Several environment factors were validated as having impact on bank efficiency such as financial regulation &amp; liberalization, competition level within the banking industry, acquisitions, ownership, and risk), the impact on BE was assessed using deployment of Malmquist Productivity Change Index, SFA, SDF, as well as DEA and improved DEA models. (2) Despite including many possible external factors impacting Bank performance, Oil Price volatility was not included in the list of studied variables as having impact on financial industries precisely banking or even economic growth. (3) A broad range of methods had been used to assess the effect of environmental variables on BE. Earlier work deployed the observe-and-compare technique by grouping the sample banks. In summary, the criteria of selected factors impacting bank efficiency were derived from various previous studies that focused on some of these factors.</p>

 <div id="s0002-s2002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i7">2.2. Oil price volatility</h3>
  <p>Oil price volatility (OPV) refers to the degree of increase or decrease in the price of crude oil over a given time interval caused by demand and supply factors, deviation from anticipated limits, or even a result of some political disturbances in the countries providing the biggest share of oil exports (Poghosyan &amp; Hesse, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0087" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2009</a></span>). OPV’s most substantial effect has a macroeconomic dimension (stock market &amp; monetary system). Hamilton (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0035" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1996</a></span>) and Lee et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0069" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1995</a></span>) used asymmetric non-linear transformations of OPV, in which Hamilton (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0036" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003</a></span>) asserted that the linear link of economic progress with oil price is unexpected. Gisser and Goodwin (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0031" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1986</a></span>) reported how US economy is directly impacted by an important factor that is crude oil price, while Mork et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0076" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1994</a></span>) found significantly negative link between oil price hike and gross domestic product (GDP) among seven Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) countries (the US, Canada, the UK, France, West Germany, Japan, and Norway), except for Norway—an oil-reliant country. In assessing this link across six Asian countries, Cunado and De Gracia (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0024" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005</a></span>) found a significant effect of oil price on price indices and economic activity. Berument et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0011" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2010</a></span>) noted a positive and statistically significant link between OPV and economic progress in Middle East North Africa (MENA) countries—net oil exporters. Studies like Alqahtani et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0005" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), concluded that the oil prices positively affect the bank indices up to the $95 per barrel mark. Ehouman (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0026" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), concluded that the if American banks are exposed to the oil market, they can influence the oil as well as financial markets. Profits are positively impacted by the positive oil price movement and if oil interrelates with the income other than the interest income then the relationship becomes stronger (Killins &amp; Mollick, ; Lee &amp; Lee, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0068" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2019</a></span>; Ma et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0071" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2021</a></span>). Saif-Alyousfi et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0089" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2020</a></span>), concluded that the oil and gas prices have direct impact on the banks performance especially in the GCC countries.</p>
  <p>Many studies provided empirical evidence on the effect of OPV on inflation levels and how this inflation can harm economic output or GDP (Hamilton, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0033" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1983</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0034" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1988</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0035" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1996</a></span>; Hooker, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0041" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1996</a></span>, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0042" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1999</a></span>; Huntington, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0043" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1998</a></span>; Kahn &amp; Hampton, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0056" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1990</a></span>; Mork, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0075" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1989</a></span>; Mork et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0076" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1994</a></span>; Tatom, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0098" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1988</a></span>) with some factors, such as monetary policies, business cycle, as well as interest &amp; exchange rates. Based on co-integrated vector auto-regressive (VAR) framework for Group of Seven (G7) countries, Cologni and Manera (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0023" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2008</a></span>) found OPV increased inflation. Using two proxies, Cunado and De Gracia (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0024" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005</a></span>) tested the effect of OPV on inflation, in which higher impact was noted when oil price was measured with oil price index than national real price currency, attributable to exchange rate.</p>
  <p>Uri (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0099" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1996</a></span>) reported a stable link between agricultural employment and OPV for 1947–1997. Similar outcome was noted by Guo and Kliesen (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0032" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2005</a></span>) for OPV impact on US macroeconomic aspects (fixed investment, employment, &amp; consumption) for 1984–2004. Papapetrou (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0081" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2001</a></span>) assessed the dynamic correlations among employment, real oil prices and stock return, as well as industrial production in Greece. As a result, the effect of oil price was significant upon employment and economic activities.</p>
  <p>Chen and Chen (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0018" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2007</a></span>) revealed a co-integrating correlation between real exchange rate and real oil price after assessing monthly panel data retrieved for 1972–2005 from G7 countries. By employing the cash flow dividend valuation model, Jones and Kaul (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0048" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1996</a></span>) assessed international stock market reaction towards OPV in the US, Canada, Japan, and the UK. Despite the inconclusive outcomes for the UK and Japan, the reaction of US and Canadian stock market was affected by OPV on cash flow. A positive link was noted between OPV and bank profitability when Poghosyan and Hesse (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0087" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2009</a></span>) used ROA financial ratio to examine dynamic panel data as profitability is persistent in nature—the dependent variable.</p>
 </div>

<div id="s0003" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i8" class="section-heading-2">3. Data and methodology</h2>
 <p>This study is based on a data set of a sample of 112 banks and 1,792 observations within BRICS Countries (Brazil, Russia, India, China, and south Africa) for the time interval 2003–2018. The study adopts the non-parametric data model as it presents many benefits precisely the Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA), which can handle unfixed number of output meaning that the selected sample should not follow a specific distribution or be transformed, which is not the case when using parametric methods, and can provide valuable information for the projection of the inefficient decision-making units (DMUs) on the efficient frontier (Charnes et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0016" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1985</a></span>; Banker et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0008" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1984</a></span>)</p>
 <p>A two-stage methodology is applied to process the empirical results consisting in using the Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) in order to identify the scores of banks efficiency at a first stage (Stage 1) and determining how volatility in Oil price has impact on these scores of efficiencies on a second stage (Stage 2). The scores of BE (from Stage 1) are regressed over variables that represent environmental and Oil price volatility variables.</p>
 <p>As for Oil price data, the paper used “World Bank” database to obtain oil price and country-level aggregate data.</p>
 <div id="s0003-s2001" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">
  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i9">3.1. Stage 1: Estimating efficiency using DEA</h3>
  <p>We adopt DEA methodology in this research to compute DEA efficiency scores. The estimated efficiency score can be broken down into three categories: (1) Technical efficiency (TE), (2) pure technical efficiency (PTE) and (3) scale efficiency (SE). It was Farrell (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0028" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1957</a></span>) work that established the DEA method of evaluating efficiency, followed by Charnes et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0017" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1978</a></span>) and Banker et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0008" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1984</a></span>).</p>
 </div>
 <div id="s0003-s2002" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_2">

  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i16">3.3. The dependent variable “Bank efficiency”</h3>
  <p>Among the approaches that have been proposed for assessing input-output links, there are two major categories:</p>
  <p>First, the “production approach” that defines banks as providers or producers of liquidity within loanable funds markets and that was used by many authors (Sathye (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0091" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2001</a></span>), Neal (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0078" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2004</a></span>), and Paul and Kourouche (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0085" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2008</a></span>)). Alternative conceptualizations are used in this approach to describe inputs (capital costs, labour, and deposits) and outputs (financial investment &amp; loans; Fried et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0030" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1993</a></span>; Kirkwood &amp; Nahm, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0063" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>). A further way of conceptualizing inputs and outputs is as expenditures and revenues, respectively (Miller &amp; Noulas, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0074" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1996</a></span>).</p>
  <p>Second, the” bank intermediation approach” derived from the intermediation role the banks occupy in any economy which is the medium point between lenders and borrowers or in other words providers of funds and demanders of these funds. As for precising which set of data will serve as the input factors and which are to serve as the output factor in this approach, it is noted that liabilities of a bank extracted from its balance sheet (which means deposit accounts belonging to providers of funds) are all set to be the input factors, and assets of a bank (which means loans granted to demanders of funds) are set to be the output factors.</p>
  <p>The present study adopts the “intermediation approach” where the first input is denoted (X1) and non-interest expenses or the second input is denoted (X2) from one side. On a parallel side, net interest income or first output is denoted (y1), and non-interest income or second output is denoted (y2).</p>
  <p>In order to convert nominal values into constant 2010 USD, data was deflated using the consumer price index. A correlation analysis of input–output relationships was conducted (and is presented in Table <button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="t0002">2</button> of the results section) to justify their inclusion in the model. It is vital to satisfy isotonicity demands in terms of both outputs and inputs (e.g., more inputs lead to higher outputs), according to Charnes et al. (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0016" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1985</a></span>).</p>

  <h3 class="section-heading-3" id="_i21">4.1. Stage 1: DEA of bank efficiency results</h3>
  <p>As shown in Table <button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="t0006">6</button>, Stage 1 results (DEA) are summarized. Building up on these results, it can be deducted that the BRICS countries’ banks performed relatively well over the period 2003–2018.  <h2 id="_i25" class="section-heading-2">5. Conclusions and recommendations</h2>
 <p>The two-stage DEA was used in this paper to evaluate the impact of OPV on BE within the context of BRICS countries from 2003 to 2018, mainly to define the country- and bank-specific factors of BE with the use of balanced panel data. This study first assessed the efficiency of 112 banks over 16-year period, and later investigated the effect of oil price volatility PV on banking efficiency.</p>
 <p>We applied the dynamic panel data approach in this study and we examined the influence of other factors on technical efficiency levels so that policy makers and bankers can improve their management of regulatory policies. Therefore, the technique prescribed by Papke and Wooldridge (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0082" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1996</a></span>) was applied. The estimation method introduced by Papke and Wooldridge (<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="cit0082" data-reflink="_i26 _i27 _i28" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1996</a></span>) eliminates the necessity of adjusting the dependent variables at extreme values of 0 or 1, in cases where the independent variables can be estimated in a straightforward manner. As a result, the predicted value of the dependent variable should range from 0 to 1. To implement this, we used the Bernoulli log-likelihood function. We included the effect of financial crisis in our analysis by subdividing the full period into two sub-periods: before crisis and after crisis periods. We also considered the effect of internal (bank-specific characteristics) as well as external bank factors (macroeconomic, regulatory, and institutional variables).</p>
 <p>In summary, the findings of the study suggest that the overall efficacy of the banks in the BRCIS has received 76% efficiency which is considered as good. It implies that with the consistency in the output the banks are expected to save up to 24% in operating cost. Therefore, the overall efficiency of the banks in BRICS countries can be considered as good during the period.</p>
 <p>Oil price volatility affected BE in BRICS countries with inflation and economic progress serving as the primary indirect drivers. Considering credit risk indicator, LNP exemplified a significantly negative effect on BE. Evidently, banking efficiency was better with lower ratio of loan loss reserves to total loans. Expected loss by banks were reflected by loan loss reserves, while higher LNP depicted larger fraction of bank assets put aside for loan repayment defaults. High loss of loans led to lower BE, particularly among banks in BRICS countries that heavily rely on loan generation. The findings of the study have several implications for the bakers, government, customers, and portfolio investors. It is expected to help the bankers in making key managerial decisions to increase the investors wealth. Governments in BRICS countries can use it as policy documents to measure the possible changes in banks efficiency due to change in oil prices. However, it is recommended for future research and empirical studies to provide detailed investigation of impact of oil price changes on baking efficiency throughout different sub-periods, in order to capture the dynamic alteration in reaction of banking sector performance to potential structural breaks and changes in oil price behavior.</p>
