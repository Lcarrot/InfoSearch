<div id="S0005" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec-type_intro NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i6" class="section-heading-2">Introduction</h2>
 <p>Falls are the most common cause of traumatic morbidity and mortality in the elderly and are responsible for hospitalization in up to one in three people over the age of 65 years.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0001" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0002" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2</a></span></sup> Falls also account for 5% of ocular injuries, of which open-globe (OG) injuries pose the most serious threat to vision. According to the United States Eye Injury Registry, fall mechanism and age &gt;60 years increased the likelihood of blindness, defined as best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) worse than 20/200.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0003" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>3</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0004" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>4</a></span></sup></p>
 <p>In our anecdotal experience, visual outcomes are poor in pseudophakic geriatric patients with fall-related OG injuries, particularly with extracapsular cataract extraction (ECCE) wound dehiscence, but prognostic data are limited. Few reports have addressed geriatric and fall-related OG injuries in general.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>5</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span></sup> A report of phakic and pseudophakic geriatric OG injuries (all mechanisms) demonstrated worse initial and follow-up BCVA and lower ocular trauma score (OTS) compared to their younger counterparts.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>5</a></span></sup> In a report of OG injuries resulting from falls in phakic and pseudophakic patients of all ages, fall-related OG injuries resulted in worse initial and postoperative BCVA, and they occurred more frequently in older patients than in the control database of OG injuries.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span></sup></p>
 <p>The magnitude of the public health and economic consequences of fall-related ocular injuries will increase as the aging population grows. The aim of this study therefore was to investigate the characteristics, visual and surgical outcomes, and prognostic factors of fall-related OG injuries in pseudophakic patients.</p>
</div>
<div id="S0006" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec-type_methods NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i7" class="section-heading-2">Methods</h2>
 <p>The study protocol was approved by the Human Investigations Committee of the Yale University School of Medicine. A retrospective chart review was performed for all patients who presented to the Yale–New Haven Hospital with OG injury between January 1, 2003, and December 31, 2013. The hospital and outpatient records were reviewed for the selection of cases and tabulation of demographics, ophthalmic history, circumstances and mechanism of injury; BCVA and ocular examination findings at the time of injury; and surgical interventions and the follow-up BCVA and ocular examination findings. Fall-related OG injuries in pseudophakic patients with at least 6 months of follow-up after injury were identified for inclusion in the study.</p>
 <p>OTS was calculated as described previously for each patient.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0007" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>7</a></span></sup> We used unpaired <i>t</i>-tests to compare mean ± standard deviation (SD) of the OTS between patients with 1) history of ECCE versus standard phacoemulsification (PE), 2) history of penetrating keratoplasty (PKP) versus no PKP, 3) final BCVA ≥20/40 versus &lt;20/40, 4) final BCVA ≥20/400 versus &lt;20/400, and 5) phthisis at final follow-up versus no phthisis. Mean (± SD) of wound size (in millimeters) was also compared among the aforementioned groups.</p>
 <p>Fisher’s exact tests were used to determine if significant associations existed between predictor variables and outcomes. Predictors were presence of layering hyphema (any size), total hyphema, 360° subconjunctival hemorrhage (SCH), intraocular lens (IOL) damage, IOL expulsion, vitreous hemorrhage, retinal detachment (RD), choroidal detachment or rupture, wound size ≥10 mm, uveal prolapse, vitreous prolapse, corneal wound (Zone I),<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0008" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>8</a></span></sup> corneoscleral wound within 5 mm of the limbus (Zone II), corneoscleral wound extending ≥5 mm from the limbus (Zone III), future RD, and future proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR). Outcomes were final BCVA ≥20/40, final BCVA ≥20/400, final BCVA &lt;20/400, no light perception at final follow-up, phthisis at final follow-up, ≥2 surgeries needed during follow-up, and ≥3 surgeries needed during follow-up. An odds ratio &gt;1 (correlative) or &lt;1 (inverse) with <i>P</i>≤0.05 was considered significant. Statistical analyses were performed using SAS 9.3.</p>
</div>
<div id="S0007" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec-type_results NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i8" class="section-heading-2">Results</h2>
 <p>Inclusion criteria were met by 26 pseudophakic patients, 19 of whom (73%) were women. Mean age (± SD) was 80.6±4.6 years (range: 61–97 years). Cataract surgery had been performed 4.6±3.4 years (range: 1.5–14.0 years) prior to the date of OG injury, and the procedure was PE in 16 patients (62%) and ECCE in 10 patients (38%). Ocular history also included PKP in four patients (15%) and glaucoma surgery in two patients (8%). Four patients (15%) had dementia, and a majority of patients had at least one or a combination of cardiovascular comorbidities, including hypertension (88%), diabetes (54%), and coronary artery disease (38%).</p>
 <p>Falls occurred predominantly indoors and at home. The most common materials of impact were the floor, furniture, and sharp objects (<button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="T0001">Table 1</button>). The left eye was injured in 14 of 26 cases (54%). BCVA at the time of injury was ≤20/200 in all patients and was &lt;20/400 in 24 of 25 patients (96%). One patient could not be tested for visual acuity due to profound dementia.</p>

 <p></p>
 <p>Hyphema was present in 22 patients (85%), 77% of whom had ≥50% anterior chamber (AC) fill. All 26 patients had iris damage, including uveal prolapse in 85% of patients. Approximately half of the patients presented with 360° SCH. Damage to the IOL included expulsed or dislocated IOLs in 58% of patients.</p>
 <p>Adnexal injuries (periorbital ecchymosis and edema, facial laceration, and orbital fracture) and posterior injuries (vitreous hemorrhage, RD, and choroidal hemorrhage or detachment) were variably present at the time of globe rupture (<button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="T0001">Table 1</button>). Wound location was predominant in Zones I and II; Zone III wounds occurred in six patients (23%). There were no cases of intraocular foreign body.</p>
 <p>Mean OTS was 38.54±10.95. OTS was significantly lower in patients with 6-month and final BCVA &lt;20/40, &lt;20/400, and phthisis, compared to patients with BCVA ≥20/40, 20/400, and no phthisis, respectively (<i>P</i>=0.0017, <i>P</i>&lt;0.0001, and <i>P</i>=0.0240; <button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="T0002">Table 2</button> and <a href="#F0001">Figure 1</a>). Wound size was significantly larger in the same categories (<i>P</i>=0.0440, 0.0145, and 0.0026; <button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="T0003">Table 3</button> and <a href="#F0002">Figure 2</a>). There was no significant difference in OTS or wound size between patients with history of ECCE versus standard PE or between those with PKP versus no PKP. Times to presentation and primary repair were within 24 hours for 24 of 26 patients.</p>

 <p></p>
 <p>Mean final follow-up was 16.6±14.7 months (range: 6–48 months). BCVA at 6 months and at final follow-up was &lt;20/400 in 18 of 25 eyes (72%). Twelve eyes (46%) required ≥2 surgeries after injury, including vitrectomy at a later date for cases that involved vitreous loss, nonclearing vitreous hemorrhage, or posterior injury. Seven eyes (26%) developed phthisis, and enucleation was performed on one eye (2%). There were no cases of endophthalmitis, and all patients had received intravenous antibiotics at the time of OG repair.</p>
 <p>Fisher’s exact tests revealed decreased likelihood of 6-month and final BCVA ≥20/40 in patients with wound size ≥10 mm and RD at the time of injury (<i>P</i>=0.0491, <button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="T0004">Table 4</button>). Patients with 360° SCH, wound size ≥10 mm, RD at initial presentation, delayed RD, or delayed PVR were less likely to achieve BCVA ≥20/400 at 6 months and at final follow-up (<i>P</i>=0.0498, <i>P</i>=0.0181, <i>P</i>=0.0022, <i>P</i>&lt;0.0001, or <i>P</i>=0.0097, respectively; <button class="ref showTableEventRef" data-id="T0004">Table 4</button>). The number of required surgical interventions was more likely to be ≥2 in patients who presented with total hyphema, IOL damage, and IOL expulsion (<i>P</i>=0.0345, <i>P</i>&lt;0.0001, and <i>P</i>=0.0023).</p>

 <h2 id="_i11" class="section-heading-2">Discussion</h2>
 <p>While traumatic OG injuries are typically associated with young males,<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0009" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>9</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>10</a></span></sup> fall-related OG injury represents a demographically and clinically unique entity. Falls are the most common mechanism of OG injury in geriatric patients<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0003" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>3</a></span></sup> and in pseudophakic wound dehiscence.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>11</a></span></sup> Women with osteoporosis and osteoarthritis face increased fall risk, and as was the case in our study, patients often have significant medical comorbidities, rendering the potential for the fall to be a preterminal event.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span></sup> Our cases also confirm prior reports of increased prevalence of fall-related OG injuries in elderly women,<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>5</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>10</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>11</a></span></sup> tendency for blunt rupture mechanism,<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>10</a></span></sup> and poor visual outcome.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>10</a></span></sup><sup>–</sup><sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0012" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>12</a></span></sup></p>
 <p>Although ocular trauma in the domestic setting is underreported,<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0009" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>9</a></span></sup> our cases further suggest that fall-related OG injuries are most commonly sustained at home.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>5</a></span></sup> The surgical wound in pseudophakic patients appears to render the eye susceptible to blunt rupture.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>5</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>11</a></span></sup> In a review of 63 patients with pseudophakic wound dehiscence caused by falls, blunt trauma, and motor vehicle accidents, history of standard PE was associated with better final BCVA compared to history of ECCE.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>11</a></span></sup> In contrast, 14 of our 26 fall cases involved larger ECCE or PKP wounds, but neither of these entities was associated with decreased OTS, increased wound size, or decreased final BCVA compared to standard PE.</p>
 <p>While the majority of injuries in the United States Eye Injury Registry demonstrate eventual recovery of functional visual acuity,<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0003" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>3</a></span></sup> visual acuity in our series was poor both at initial presentation and at final follow-up, including final BCVA &lt;20/400 in 72% of eyes and hand motion vision or worse in more than half. Similarly, final BCVA was &lt;20/400 in 84% of patients with fall-related OG injuries.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span></sup> In a study of geriatric OG injuries, lower OTS and worse initial and final visual acuity levels were noted in comparison to OG injuries in younger patients.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>5</a></span></sup> In our patients, lower OTS, larger wound size, 360° SCH, RD, and PVR were predictive of poor visual outcome, while hyphema, iris injury, IOL damage, adnexal injury, vitreous hemorrhage, and wound location did not yield significant results. The average OTS in this pseudophakic fall population was 38.54 (SD: 10.95); thus, most patients were OTS Class 1 or 2, with &gt;50% chance of final BCVA being light perception or worse.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0007" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>7</a></span></sup> Concordantly, 52% of our patients had light perception or worse at 6 months and at final follow-up.</p>
 <p>Posterior injuries were present in a majority of patients, while adnexal injuries (including periorbital ecchymosis or edema) and facial or eyelid laceration were important presenting signs and were more common in this series than in a population of all mechanisms of OG injury.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0013" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>13</a></span></sup> There was 100% incidence of iris damage, which in conjunction with a limbal wound, predisposes the pseudophakic patient to hyphema and often bullous SCH.</p>
 <p>Enucleation and endophthalmitis have been uncommon outcomes in this population, likely due to blunt (rather than penetrating) OG injury mechanism and the absence of intraocular foreign body.<sup><span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>5</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>6</a></span>,<span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0014" data-reflink="_i13 _i14" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>14</a></span></sup> However, phthisis tended to develop in patients with lower OTS and larger wound size. Approximately half of the patients required ≥2 surgeries after injury, with IOL expulsion or dislocation and total hyphema being predictive factors. Limitations to this study include retrospective design and relatively small sample size due to the very specific nature of the injury and population. Furthermore, the presence of dementia in this population also limits the ability to obtain an accurate visual acuity.</p>
 <p>In conclusion, fall-related OG injury in pseudophakic patients results in profound visual disability, with damage to multiple ocular structures. Wound size, OTS, hyphema, IOL damage, and posterior injury are important prognostic considerations. Despite vision being maximized in the pseudophakic population, the geriatric patient remains at risk for falls, particularly with previously addressed risk factors. Because visual loss is not a commonly discussed consequence of falls, it is incumbent upon the ophthalmologist, who deals primarily with the geriatric population, to contribute to the discussion of fall risk and direct the pseudophakic patient to appropriate educational resources.</p>
