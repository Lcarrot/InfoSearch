 <h2 id="_i4" class="section-heading-2">1. Introduction</h2>
 <p>The induction motor (IM) and its control have been widely researched in the last three decades due to many reasons; the IM is less expensive compared to other motors types, so the IM can be used in a wide rang of applications such as heavy lifting, wind turbine and electrical vehicle. In addition, the maintenance cost can be neglected in the IM due to its construction. In other words, one can notice that a wide range of industrial drive applications use IMs (Buja &amp; Kazmierkowski, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0002" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2</a></span>; Leonhard, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0012" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2001</a></span>) than other motor types. These drivers have been designed and built using the “modern” or vector modeling method. The IM vector modeling depends on two reference frames; stationary and arbitrary, which were originally introduced by Clark and Park using well-known 3-to-2 phase transformations (Clarke, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0007" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1943</a></span>; Park, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0015" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>15</a></span>). Theses transformations as well as field orientation control (FOC) technique lead to an IM model in which we can deal with the IM similar to the DC motor (Holtz, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0010" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>10</a></span>). However, engineers always demand more efficient industrial drives, i.e., fast, simple and robust performance, for the IM application.</p>
 <p>On the other hand a nonlinear model predictive control (NMPC) is one of the most important control algorithms, since we can employ an explicit nonlinear model as well as constraints on state and control variables (Diehl, Findeisen, &amp; Schwarzkopf et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0008" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2002</a></span>; Hong, Wang, Li, &amp; Wozny et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0011" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2006</a></span>; Qin &amp; Badgwell, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0016" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003</a></span>). (Chang &amp; Kim, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0005" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>1997</a></span>) have suggested a minimum-time minimum-loss speed control algorithm for IMs under FOC with IM variable constraints to obtain high performance and efficiency. (Miranda, Cort´Es, &amp; Yuz et al., <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0013" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2009</a></span>) presented a predictive control algorithm that uses a state-space model.</p>
 <p>Scoltock <i>et al</i> <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0018" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2010</a></span>have compared two MPC of IM. Namely, forced machine current control which was proposed in the early 1980s and model predictive direct torque/current control. They show through a simulation proposal that the steady-state performance of both methods is similar when the switching horizon of second method is limited. However, when the switching horizon is extended, the performance of the second method is improved. Wang and Gan (Wang &amp; Gan, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0022" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2013</a></span>) have proposed the design of gain scheduled continuous-time MPC with constraints. They used the linearized model of induction machine example to illustrate this controller.</p>
 <p>The conventional field orientation condition concept is to make the IM emulate the separately excited DC motor as a source of adjustable torque. This will be done by assuming one of the flux components equals to zero while the motor rotating and then one can easily obtain the needed frequency for the motor operating point. However, in this work we use the original nonlinear model of the IM to formulate the nonlinear optimal control problem. That is, both flux components are not assumed to be zeros, but they are considered state variables in the NMPC problem. On the other hand, the control variables will then be the input frequency and source voltages (in the voltage-fed case) or the frequency and source currents (in the current-fed case).</p>

 <h2 id="_i13" class="section-heading-2">3. Model predictive control of the IM</h2>
 <p>MPC is a control strategy therewith we compute, online, the plant control that optimize (minimize) certain performance index subject to model equations and constrains involving states and controls (Bartholomew Biggs, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0001" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2008</a></span>; Chen, O’Reilly, &amp; Ballance, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0006" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2003</a></span>; Tamimi &amp; Li, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0019" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2009</a></span>). Therefore, the model is used to predict the process output at a future time horizon by finding an optimal control sequence for a predefined objective functional using the theory of the optimal control (Camacho &amp; Alba, <span class="ref-lnk lazy-ref"><a data-rid="CIT0003" data-reflink="_i42" href="#"><span class="off-screen">Citation</span>2013</a></span>).</p>
 <p>The system to be controlled is explicitly described by a set of differential algebraic equations (DAEs), e.g., (1) to (5) for the IM case, with initial conditions that are measured or estimated before optimization. Again, the optimal control is then formulated by defining a performance index to be minimized subject to the set of the DAEs, initial conditions as well as box constraints on the state and control variables.  <h2 id="_i23" class="section-heading-2">6. Model predictive control of the voltage-fed IM</h2>
 <p>In the voltage-fed IM case, the control signal for a voltage source inverter are the stator voltages. In this case, all <a href="#M0001">Equations (1)</a>–(5) are considered. This means, the stator currents, rotor voltages as well as rotor speed are considered as system states where the synchronous speed or excitation frequency is third control command that will be fed to the voltage source inverter. Figure <a href="#F0004">4</a> shows block diagram of the IM adjustable drive control using NMPC with voltage-fed commands.</p>

<div id="S0009" class="NLM_sec NLM_sec_level_1">
 <h2 id="_i40" class="section-heading-2">8. Conclusion</h2>
 <p>In this paper, NMPC is applied to control the IM. Therefore, the formulated optimal control problem (OCP) is repeatedly solved using NMPC. This OCP is first formulated using the third order IM model, thus, the system states are the fluxes in the arbitrary rotating frame as well as the motor speed where the controls are the stator currents and input frequency. The OCP is formulated using the fifth order model, i.e., the system states are the fluxes in the arbitrary rotating frame, the stator currents in the stator frame and the motor speed where the systems controls are the terminal stator voltage in the stator frame and the input frequency. These nonlinear OCPs are solved using direct method. Moreover, stabilization constraints are added the OCPs to ensure the stability of the motor speed. Simulation results show the effectiveness of the proposed controller in both cases where the motor speed reaches the desired speed after 0.2&nbsp;s.</p>
